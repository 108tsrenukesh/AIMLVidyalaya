Idea 1

What even is forecasting?

The eternal question: how many samosas?
🧒
Like you're 10

A forecast is just a really good guess about the future. Like guessing how many samosas your relatives will eat at a party. Guess too few and people are sad and hungry. Guess too many and you're eating cold samosas for breakfast all week. Forecasting is the art of guessing just right.

🛒
In the real shop

A snack company has to decide how many packets to make and ship to each town, every week, for thousands of products. Make too few — empty shelves, lost sales, angry stores. Make too many — money frozen in warehouses, snacks going stale. The "samosa guess" is now millions of guesses a week, and getting them slightly better is worth crores.

🎓
What the scientists call it

A demand forecast is a probability distribution over future quantities — not one number, but a whole range of "how likely is each amount." We usually report a middle estimate (point forecast) plus a band of uncertainty (prediction interval), at a specific level (this SKU, this depot, next week), because that's what the stocking decision actually consumes.

😄 A forecast is a guess wearing a lab coat. The lab coat is the maths. The guess is still a guess — we just make it an honest, measured one.

Idea 2

The wonderfully lazy baseline

"Same as last time" — annoyingly hard to beat
🧒
Like you're 10

The easiest guess in the world: "however much we sold last time, we'll sell that again." Sold 10 ice creams last Sunday? Guess 10 for this Sunday. It feels too simple to be useful — but it's right surprisingly often, because the future usually looks a lot like the recent past.

🛒
In the real shop

"Last festival we sold 820 boxes, so plan for ~820 this festival." Every clever model a company builds has to beat this lazy guess to be worth the trouble. And here's the embarrassing secret of the industry: a shocking number of expensive forecasting systems quietly do worse than "same as last year" — they just never checked.

🎓
What the scientists call it

This is the seasonal-naive forecast: ŷ for a period = the actual value one full season ago. It's the mandatory baseline — the floor every model must clear on honest backtesting. It even doubles as the yardstick inside the MASE metric (MASE < 1 means "we beat naive"). Rule one of forecasting: always measure against it.

😄 The baseline is the forecasting version of wearing the same outfit because it worked last time. Lazy? Yes. Effective? Also annoyingly yes.

Idea 3

There are patterns hiding in the mess

A wiggly sales line is really a few simple things stacked up
🧒
Like you're 10

Ice cream sells more in summer. Umbrellas sell more in the monsoon. That's a pattern — the same shape repeats every year. If you spot the pattern, your guesses get way better. Most "random" sales aren't random at all; they're a slow trend, plus a repeating seasonal wave, plus festival bumps, plus a little bit of genuine chaos.

🛒
In the real shop

Cold-drink sales climb every summer and crash every winter; namkeen spikes before Diwali; salaries hit accounts and demand jumps for a few days. A planner who separates "the steady trend" from "the yearly wave" from "the festival spike" can forecast each piece and add them back together — far better than staring at one jagged line.

🎓
What the scientists call it

This is time-series decomposition: y = trend + seasonality + calendar/event effects + noise (additively, or multiplicatively when swings scale with volume). Every model family is really a different bet about which of these dominates and how they interact — and the irreducible "noise" is the hard limit on how good any forecast can ever be.

😄 A sales chart looks like a heart-rate monitor during a horror movie. Decomposition is putting on the glasses that reveal it was three calm things wearing a trench coat.

Idea 4

Two ways to guess: a wise elder vs the whole bazaar

Classical models remember; ML models learn from everyone at once
🧒
Like you're 10

Two ways to guess tomorrow's ice-cream sales. Way 1: ask the wise old shopkeeper who's watched this stall for years — they just know its rhythm. Way 2: peek at every stall in the city at once and learn the rules they all share, so even a brand-new stall benefits from everyone else's experience. Way 2 is newer and usually wins when there are lots of stalls.

🛒
In the real shop

The "wise elder" is great for one clean, well-behaved product. But a company has thousands of products — many with thin sales histories. Training one model across all of them lets a sleepy product borrow the pattern of its busy cousins (the same brand, the same category). That sharing is exactly why machine learning took over retail forecasting, and it can also use levers like price and promotions that the elder can't.

🎓
What the scientists call it

The "elder" = classical models (ETS, ARIMA) — one model per series, few parameters, native uncertainty. The "bazaar" = cross-learning / global models, where one model (usually gradient-boosted trees like LightGBM, or deep nets) trains across all series and ingests covariates. Cross-learning swept the M5 retail competition. Above that sit deep architectures and pretrained foundation models — same idea, more horsepower.

😄 The wise elder gives you a thoughtful answer about one product. The bazaar gives you a slightly-less-thoughtful answer about a million products before lunch. Guess which one the supply chain prefers.

Idea 5

The lonely products that barely sell

Mostly zeros, occasional spike — and a dying product that must fade to zero
🧒
Like you're 10

Some products are introverts. They sit on the shelf for weeks doing nothing, then boop — someone buys three. Saying "this sells 0.3 units a week on average" is useless: you can't sell a third of a thing. These shy items need a totally different kind of guessing — about how often a sale happens and how big it is when it does.

🛒
In the real shop

Half a real catalogue is this long, lumpy tail — slow movers, spare parts, niche flavours. And some are quietly dying (being discontinued). A naive method keeps cheerfully predicting sales for a product nobody buys anymore, so you over-stock a corpse. You want a method that notices the silence and lets the forecast fade toward zero.

🎓
What the scientists call it

This is intermittent demand. You classify each item by ADI (how often it sells) and CV² (how erratic the size) into smooth / intermittent / erratic / lumpy, then route to the right method: Croston and the de-biased SBA for stable intermittency, and TSB when obsolescence is a risk — because TSB updates a demand probability every week, so a dying item decays to zero. And never score these with MAPE.

😄 Croston is the friend who only texts when they want something. TSB is the friend who notices when you've stopped texting back and gracefully fades away. Be like TSB.

Idea 6

Making all the numbers add up

If you guess each level separately, they won't agree — so you fix them
🧒
Like you're 10

Imagine guessing how much pocket money your whole family spends, AND how much each person spends, separately. Problem: when you add up everyone's guesses, the total won't match your "whole family" guess! Someone has to gently nudge all the numbers so the little ones add up to the big one. That nudging is the trick.

🛒
In the real shop

A company forecasts at many levels — national, zone, city, channel (general trade / modern trade / e-commerce), SKU. Leadership commits to the national number; each depot stocks to the local one. If they don't tie out, planning and finance are looking at different "truths." So you reconcile them into one coherent set that adds up at every level.

🎓
What the scientists call it

Hierarchical forecasting & reconciliation. Options: bottom-up (sum the leaves), top-down (split the total), middle-out, and the modern default MinT (minimum-trace), which optimally combines forecasts from all levels using their error covariance — usually more accurate and guaranteed coherent. The frontier even reconciles across time frequencies (weekly ⇄ monthly ⇄ yearly) at once.

😄 It's the family WhatsApp group of forecasting: everyone has their own version of events, and someone has to make the numbers agree before the meeting.

Idea 7

How wrong were we? (and do we always guess low?)

Two different kinds of "wrong": scatter (noise) vs a consistent lean (bias)
🧒
Like you're 10

If you keep guessing how tall your friend is, two things can go wrong. You might be all over the place — sometimes too high, sometimes too low (just noisy). Or you might always guess too short — that's a lean, a habit, a bias. Noisy is annoying; always-too-low is dangerous, because it's a mistake you make every single time.

🛒
In the real shop

If your forecasts are always a bit low, you constantly run out of stock and lose sales — quietly, forever. The sneaky part: your "average error" can look fine while one region is always over and another always under, cancelling out on paper while both bleed money. So you check error and the lean, sliced by region and channel.

🎓
What the scientists call it

Two things you must report together: magnitude (use WMAPE for retail — total error ÷ total volume, which survives the zeros that break MAPE; MASE to compare across series) and bias (the signed mean error — is it systematically high or low?). And you measure it with rolling-origin backtesting, never one lucky month.

😄 Low bias with high noise is a clumsy friend who means well. Low noise with high bias is a friend who lies to you very consistently. The second one is worse.

Idea 8

Don't pack for the average day

The average is one number; demand is a whole range of possibilities
🧒
Like you're 10

The average rainfall in your city might be "a little." But if you only pack for the average day, you'll be soaked on the big-storm days. Smart people pack an umbrella for the worst likely day, not the typical one. Demand is the same: planning for the average means you run out exactly when it matters.

🛒
In the real shop

If you stock the average forecast, you'll be short roughly half the time — half your weeks end in stock-outs. To hit a 95% service level you deliberately stock to a higher number that covers the busy weeks too. So the useful forecast isn't a single guess; it's "here's the range, and here's the level that keeps shelves full 95% of the time."

🎓
What the scientists call it

Probabilistic forecasting: predict the whole distribution, summarised as quantiles (the P50, P90, P95…). You score it with proper rules like pinball loss and CRPS, check it's calibrated (does the "90%" band really cover 90%?), and use conformal prediction to fix over-confident intervals. The service level you target is the quantile you stock to.

😄 "On average the river is one metre deep" is famously poor comfort to the person who drowned crossing it. Forecast the range, not just the middle.

Idea 9

The point was never the forecast — it's the decision

The newspaper seller's dilemma: how many to buy this morning?
🧒
Like you're 10

A newspaper seller buys papers each morning. Buy too few → sell out and miss money. Buy too many → leftover papers are worthless tomorrow. So how many to buy depends on which mistake hurts more. If missing a sale really stings, buy extra. If leftovers are very wasteful, buy fewer. The best guess isn't the "average" — it leans toward the cheaper mistake.

🛒
In the real shop

A high-margin snack where stock-outs lose big sales? Deliberately over-stock — order a high quantile. A fresh product that spoils fast and gets marked down? Under-stock — order a low quantile. The forecast feeds this decision; the decision, not the forecast accuracy, is what actually makes or loses money. Two products with the same forecast should be ordered differently.

🎓
What the scientists call it

The newsvendor model. With under-stock cost C℅ and over-stock cost Cₒ, the optimal order is the demand quantile at the critical fractile C℅/(C℅+Cₒ). So your target service level is that fractile, and the number you forecast should be that quantile — which is exactly why pinball loss at quantile τ equals the inventory cost. Judge the system by fill rate and holding cost, not WMAPE alone.

😄 Yes, the textbook decision model in modern AI supply chains is literally named after a kid selling newspapers on a corner. The classics never die — unlike the leftover newspapers.

Idea 10

The model that has seen everything

Pretrained on millions of charts — it just "feels" where a new one goes
🧒
Like you're 10

Imagine a kid who has watched a million charts scroll by. Show them a brand-new one and they just… feel where it's heading — no studying needed, like how you can finish a song you've never heard if you've heard enough songs. That's the newest kind of model. Magical, fast, and — like any confident teenager — occasionally sure of something that's completely wrong.

🛒
In the real shop

This is brilliant for a brand-new product with no sales history — the one case where every other method is stuck. You get a sensible forecast in minutes with zero setup. But for your bread-and-butter products, where you know the prices and promotions, a carefully-built model that uses those levers still usually wins. So you keep the magic model as a fast helper and a backstop, not the whole show.

🎓
What the scientists call it

Time-series foundation models — pretrained on huge corpora to forecast new series zero-shot (TimesFM, Chronos/Chronos-2, Moirai, Lag-Llama, TimeGPT). 2025–26 brought a shared benchmark (GIFT-Eval) and covariate-aware versions — plus honest caveats: some leaderboard wins shrink on truly unseen data (benchmark leakage), and their uncertainty isn't always calibrated. Best used as a baseline, cold-start tool, and ensemble member.

😄 A foundation model is the friend who's watched every cooking show and confidently tells you how the dish ends. Right 85% of the time. The other 15%, dinner is on fire — so taste before you serve.
