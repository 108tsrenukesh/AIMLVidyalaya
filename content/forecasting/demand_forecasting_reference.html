Foundation · 8 min read

What demand forecasting really is

A demand forecast is a probability distribution over future quantities, summarised badly. Most of the craft is in deciding which summary you need, at which level, and how wrong you are allowed to be.

Strip away the tooling and demand forecasting is one question asked millions of times: how many units of this thing will sell, where, and when? The honest answer is never a single number — it is a distribution. You will sell around 4,200 cases of a snack SKU in the West zone next week, with a realistic range of maybe 3,400 to 5,100. A point forecast collapses that distribution to its mean (or median); a probabilistic forecast keeps the spread, which is what inventory and service-level decisions actually consume.

Almost every series you will forecast decomposes, at least loosely, into four parts: a trend (slow drift up or down), seasonality (repeating weekly/monthly/yearly patterns), calendar and event effects (holidays, paydays, promotions, price changes), and noise (the irreducible randomness). Different model families are really just different bets about which of these dominate and how they interact. Classical models assume the structure is additive or multiplicative and stable; machine-learning models let the structure be arbitrary but must be told about it through features; foundation models claim to have already seen enough series to recognise the structure cold.

Core intuition · decompose, model each part, recombine with uncertainty

When forecasting earns its keep — and when it doesn't

✓ Reach for a model when…

  • You have a meaningful history (rule of thumb: ≥2 full seasonal cycles) and the future resembles the past in structure.
  • The series has exploitable signal — trend, seasonality, promotions, price, weather — not just white noise.
  • The decision downstream is sensitive to the number: replenishment quantities, capacity, safety stock, S&OP commitments.
  • You can forecast at the level the decision is made (a CFA's weekly SKU demand, not just national monthly).

✗ Don't bother (or forecast differently) when…

  • The driver is a known future event you can just look up (a confirmed bulk order, a listing going live) — that's planning, not forecasting.
  • History is too short or the regime just broke (new SKU, post-merger, price reset) — use analogues or judgmental forecasts.
  • The series is pure noise around a flat mean — a moving average is your ceiling; fancier models only overfit.
  • Demand is so intermittent that "expected units per period" is the wrong question — switch to intermittent-demand methods and order-up-to logic.
The forecaster's first move is not a model — it's a baseline. Compute the seasonal naive forecast (this week = same week last year, or last week for a flat series) and measure its error. Every model you build afterwards must beat it. A startling fraction of "forecasting projects" deploy something that is, on honest backtesting, worse than seasonal naive. The scaled metrics in the Evaluation section exist precisely to keep you honest about this.

One framing worth internalising early, from the M-competitions that have shaped the field for forty years: combining simple methods and choosing the right error metric usually beats chasing a single clever model. The M5 retail competition reinforced this — the winners used ensembles of gradient-boosted trees trained across many series at once, not one bespoke deep network per product. Core methods · 16 min read

The four method tiers

In 2026 the toolkit splits into four tiers: classical statistical models, machine-learning models on engineered features, deep-learning architectures, and pretrained foundation models. Each has a place. The skill is knowing which to reach for first — and most retail teams reach for tier 2.

The general forecasting pipeline — every tier plugs into the same skeleton

Classical statistical models — ETS & ARIMA

The workhorses for decades, and still the right first call for a single series with clear trend and seasonality and a few hundred observations. Two families dominate. ETS (Error-Trend-Seasonal, i.e. exponential smoothing) models the series as smoothly evolving level, trend, and seasonal components. ARIMA models the series through its own lagged values and lagged errors after differencing to remove trend.

Exponential smoothing's core idea is a weighted average that decays into the past. Simple exponential smoothing forecasts the next value as:

ŷₜ₊₁ = α·yₜ + (1 − α)·ŷₜ ,   0 < α < 1

Holt-Winters extends this to three smoothing equations — level (ℓ), trend (b), and seasonal (s) — which is the additive-seasonal ETS model:

ℓₜ = α(yₜ − sₜ₋ₘ) + (1−α)(ℓₜ₋₁ + bₜ₋₁)
bₜ = β(ℓₜ − ℓₜ₋₁) + (1−β)bₜ₋₁
sₜ = γ(yₜ − ℓₜ₋₁ − bₜ₋₁) + (1−γ)sₜ₋ₘ

ARIMA(p,d,q) instead writes the differenced series as a linear combination of its own past and past shocks: (1−φ₁B−…−φₚBᵖ)(1−B)ᵈ yₜ = (1+θ₁B+…+θ_q B^q)εₜ, where B is the backshift operator. SARIMA adds seasonal terms. The practical magic is in automatic order selection — you almost never hand-pick p,d,q anymore; AutoARIMA searches them by minimising AICc. Hyndman & Athanasopoulos, FPP3 ↗

Strengths

  • Excellent on clean, single series with stable seasonality.
  • Few parameters, interpretable, fast, give native prediction intervals.
  • Hard to overfit; superb baselines that ML must beat.

Weaknesses

  • One model per series — doesn't scale to millions of SKUs gracefully.
  • Can't natively use covariates like price/promo (ARIMAX helps, partially).
  • Struggle with intermittency and abrupt regime change.

In 2026, you almost never implement these by hand. Nixtla's statsforecast runs AutoARIMA/AutoETS across thousands of series in parallel, fast enough to be a serious production baseline. statsforecast docs ↗

Machine learning — gradient-boosted trees (the retail default)

This is where most serious retail demand forecasting lives today, and for good reason. In the M5 competition — Walmart's hierarchical, intermittent, real sales data — practically all of the top 50 teams used LightGBM, and the winner used an equal-weighted ensemble of LightGBM models trained at different aggregation levels. M5 results ↗ Tree ensembles win because they ingest rich features (price, promo flags, calendar, weather, lags, rolling means), capture non-linear interactions, and — crucially — support cross-learning: one model trained across many series, sharing strength so that a sparse SKU borrows pattern from its category.

The reframing is the key mental shift: you turn forecasting into supervised regression. Each row is "given features known at time t, predict demand at t+h." Features are engineered to encode the time-series structure the tree can't see on its own — lagged demand (y at t−1, t−7, t−364), rolling statistics (28-day mean/std), calendar dummies, and exogenous drivers. The model never sees "time"; it sees a feature table.

Forecasting as supervised learning — the lag-feature reframe

Strengths

  • State of the art for retail/SKU forecasting at scale.
  • Natively uses price, promo, weather, calendar — the levers planners care about.
  • One model serves millions of series (cross-learning); fast training.
  • A Tweedie objective handles the zero-inflated, skewed nature of unit sales.

Weaknesses

  • Needs feature engineering — the work moves from model to features.
  • Trees can't extrapolate beyond the training range (trending series need detrending or a trend feature).
  • Recursive multi-step forecasts accumulate error; direct/per-horizon models avoid it but multiply model count.
Recursive vs direct. To forecast 8 weeks out, you can predict week 1, feed it back as a lag, predict week 2, and so on (recursive) — simple but errors compound. Or train a separate model per horizon that only uses lags old enough to be known (direct) — more robust at long horizons but 8× the models. M5 winners used both and averaged them.

Deep learning — when you have many long series

Neural architectures earn their complexity when you have many related series, long histories, and rich covariates — and when squeezing out the last few accuracy points is worth real engineering. The 2026 mainstays: N-BEATS / N-HiTS (pure deep stacks of backcast/forecast blocks, strong on univariate), DeepAR (autoregressive RNN producing probabilistic forecasts across many series), Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT) (attention-based, handles static + time-varying known/unknown covariates with interpretable variable selection), and PatchTST (a transformer that slices the series into patches, very strong on long-horizon multivariate benchmarks).

The unifying idea is the global model: a single network trained on all series learns shared temporal patterns, exactly the cross-learning principle that made LightGBM win M5, but with representation learning instead of hand-built features. TFT's distinctive contribution is gating and attention that let it pick which covariates matter at which horizon — and expose that for inspection.

Strengths

  • Best ceiling when data is abundant and covariate-rich.
  • Native multi-horizon, probabilistic (quantile) outputs.
  • TFT gives some interpretability via attention/variable selection.

Weaknesses

  • Data-hungry and compute-hungry; easy to underperform LightGBM on tabular retail data.
  • Many knobs; reproducibility and tuning cost are real.
  • Often not worth it vs a good GBM for typical FMCG panels — benchmark honestly.
Use the same library surface for all of these via Nixtla's neuralforecast or Darts, so you can swap N-HiTS for TFT for PatchTST without rewriting your harness. Darts docs ↗

Foundation models — zero-shot forecasting

The genuinely new tier. Pretrained on enormous corpora of time series, these models forecast a brand-new series with no training — you hand them history, they return a forecast, the way an LLM answers a prompt about a topic it was never explicitly taught. The 2026 set worth knowing: TimesFM (Google Research), Chronos (Amazon, tokenises values and reuses a language-model backbone), Moirai / Moirai-2 (Salesforce, any frequency/any number of variables), Lag-Llama (open, probabilistic, runs on CPU/GPU), and the API-hosted TimeGPT (Nixtla). 2026 foundation-model toolkit ↗

The promise is operational, not just accuracy: instead of fitting and maintaining a bespoke model per dataset, you orchestrate a pretrained one — faster deployment, decent generalisation, and a strong zero-shot baseline you can stand up in an afternoon. The honest caveat: on rich tabular retail data with strong promo/price signal, a well-built LightGBM still frequently beats zero-shot foundation models, and fine-tuning closes much of the rest. Treat them as a powerful baseline and ensemble member, not an automatic winner.

Strengths

  • Zero training; minutes to a credible forecast across thousands of series.
  • Strong for cold-start and short-history SKUs where you have nothing to fit.
  • Probabilistic outputs; rapidly improving on public benchmarks.

Weaknesses

  • Hard to inject domain covariates (promo/price) cleanly in pure zero-shot mode.
  • Can be beaten by a tuned GBM on the data you actually have.
  • Large models; inference cost and governance need thought.

Intermittent & lumpy demand

Most SKUs in a real catalogue don't sell every period. The long tail is full of zeros punctuated by occasional spikes. Standard models — and standard metrics — quietly break here, and the fix is a different question entirely.

When demand has many zero periods, "what is expected demand next week?" is the wrong framing, because the answer is often a fraction of a unit that never actually occurs. The classical move, from Croston (1972), is to split the problem in two: estimate the average size of demand when it occurs, and separately the average interval between occurrences, smoothing each with exponential smoothing only on periods with positive demand. The forecast rate is size divided by interval.

Croston:  forecast rate = ẑₜ / p̂ₜ  (smoothed size ÷ smoothed interval)

Croston is known to be positively biased. The Syntetos-Boylan Approximation (SBA) applies a simple correction factor that removes most of it: Teunter et al. 2011 ↗

SBA:  forecast = (1 − α/2) · (Croston forecast)

Both Croston and SBA share a blind spot: they only update when demand occurs, so during a long run of zeros — exactly what happens when an item goes obsolete — the forecast never decays. The Teunter-Syntetos-Babai (TSB) method fixes this by tracking demand probability instead of interval and updating it every period, so the forecast drifts toward zero for a dying SKU:

TSB:  forecast = p̂ₜ · ẑₜ  (demand probability × smoothed size, updated every period)

Which method? Classify the series first (ADI / CV²)

Syntetos & Boylan gave the field its standard map: classify each series by ADI (Average Demand Interval — how often demand occurs) and CV² (squared coefficient of variation of the non-zero demand sizes — how erratic the sizes are). The cutoffs ADI = 1.32 and CV² = 0.49 carve four quadrants that tell you what to use.

The ADI / CV² demand classification — pick the method from the quadrant
Never score intermittent series with MAPE. Dividing by actuals that are frequently zero makes MAPE explode or undefined; the optimiser then chases the explosions. Use scaled or volume-weighted errors (MASE, WMAPE) and — more importantly — judge intermittent items by their inventory outcome (fill rate, stock-outs, holding cost), since the point forecast is almost beside the point. See Evaluation ↗.
# Classify by ADI/CV2, then route to the right intermittent method import numpy as np from statsforecast.models import CrostonClassic, CrostonSBA, TSB, ADIDA, IMAPA def classify(y): nz = y[y > 0] if len(nz) < 2: return "new_or_dead" adi = len(y) / len(nz) cv2 = (nz.std() / nz.mean())**2 if adi < 1.32 and cv2 < 0.49: return "smooth" # ETS/ARIMA/GBM if adi < 1.32: return "erratic" # SBA / GBM if cv2 < 0.49: return "intermittent" # Croston/SBA return "lumpy" # SBA/TSB; mind obsolescence # TSB is the safe default when items can go obsolete (it decays to ~0) from statsforecast import StatsForecast sf = StatsForecast(models=[CrostonSBA(), TSB(alpha_d=0.2, alpha_p=0.2)], freq="W") fcst = sf.forecast(df=intermittent_df, h=8)

Hierarchical forecasting & reconciliation

Your forecasts live in a tree: national rolls up from zones, zones from CFAs, CFAs from channels, channels from SKUs. Forecast each level independently and they won't add up. Reconciliation makes them coherent — and, done well, more accurate too.

A hierarchy means upper levels are sums of lower levels: total = North + South + East + West; each zone = sum of its channels; each channel = sum of its SKUs. If you forecast every node independently, the children almost never sum to the parent — incoherence — which is operationally unacceptable (your national S&OP number won't match the sum of what CFAs are told to stock). Reconciliation adjusts the base forecasts so they're coherent across every level.

A demand hierarchy + the three reconciliation directions

The reconciliation methods

MethodHow it worksPros / cons
Bottom-up (BU)Forecast the leaves; sum upward.Unbiased, simple; but noisy at leaf level can hurt top-level accuracy.
Top-down (TD)Forecast the top; split down by historical or forecast proportions.Stable at top; statistically biased, weak at the bottom where decisions are made.
Middle-out (MO)Forecast a middle level; BU above, TD below.Compromise; useful when a mid level (e.g. zone) is most reliable.
MinT (optimal)Forecast every level, then optimally combine using the forecast-error covariance to minimise total variance.Usually most accurate and coherent; needs reliable error covariance estimation.

MinT (Minimum Trace) is the modern default. It treats all base forecasts as information and finds the coherent set that minimises the sum of forecast-error variances, which provably can't be worse than bottom-up if the covariance is estimated well. Wickramasuriya et al. 2019 ↗ Nixtla's hierarchicalforecast implements BU, TD, MO, MinT, ERM and probabilistic variants behind one API. hierarchicalforecast ↗

Doing it right · 10 min read

Metrics & backtesting

More forecasting reviews go wrong here than anywhere else: the wrong metric on the wrong data, judged on a single lucky split. Get the measurement right and the model choice often follows.

Two questions hide inside "how accurate is the forecast?" — how big are the errors (variance/magnitude) and do they lean one way (bias). A forecast can have small average error while systematically under-forecasting, which silently drains service levels; a different forecast can be unbiased but wildly variable. You must report both. Bias is just the signed mean error:

bias = mean(forecast − actual)  ·  negative ⇒ over-forecast, positive ⇒ under-forecast

WMAPE is the retail workhorse; MASE is the comparison referee. WMAPE answers "what % of total volume did we miss?" — exactly the planner's question — and gracefully handles the zeros MAPE chokes on. MASE puts every series on the same scale (vs seasonal naive) so a value below 1 means "we beat the baseline." Report WMAPE + bias for the business, MASE for method selection. FPP3 §5 ↗

Backtest on a rolling origin — never a single split

One held-out month can be a holiday, a stock-out, or a launch — unrepresentative. Rolling-origin cross-validation (a.k.a. time-series CV / walk-forward) repeats train→forecast→score as the cutoff marches forward, and averages. It answers the better question: "does this model perform consistently, not just once?" Always preserve time order — never shuffle — and never let a feature peek at the future (leakage).

Rolling-origin backtesting — the origin marches forward, errors averaged across folds

Business validation beyond the statistics

A good error number is necessary, not sufficient. The sanity checks practitioners actually run: (1) does it beat seasonal naive on the same folds — if not, stop and rethink; (2) is the bias near zero by segment — a model unbiased overall can be +15% on MT and −15% on GT; (3) does the forecast pass the eyeball test against the actuals plot for a sample of SKUs; (4) does it translate to inventory outcomes — for intermittent items, simulate the fill rate and stock-outs, not just point error.

# WMAPE, bias, MASE on rolling-origin CV output import numpy as np def wmape(y, yhat): return np.abs(y - yhat).sum() / np.abs(y).sum() def bias(y, yhat): return (yhat - y).mean() def mase(y, yhat, y_train, m=52): naive = np.abs(y_train[m:] - y_train[:-m]).mean() # seasonal-naive MAE in-sample return np.abs(y - yhat).mean() / naive # cv comes from sf.cross_validation(...) — already rolling-origin for model in model_cols: print(model, "WMAPE", round(wmape(cv["y"], cv[model]), 3), "bias", round(bias(cv["y"], cv[model]), 2))

Doing it right · 9 min read

Common pitfalls

The mistakes that quietly cost the most aren't exotic. They're leakage, the wrong metric, ignoring intermittency, and trusting a single backtest. Each below: what it is, why it happens, the fix — colour-coded by how much damage it does.

Diagnosis · symptom → likely cause → fix
Critical

Data leakage from the future

What: a feature contains information not available at forecast time — a rolling mean that includes the target week, a "promo" flag joined on the wrong date, target-encoded categories computed over the whole dataset. Why it happens: features are engineered on the full table before the train/test split. Fix: compute every lag/rolling feature with .shift() so it only sees the past, build features inside each CV fold, and stress-test by asking "could I actually know this value at the moment I forecast?" Leakage is the #1 reason a beautiful backtest dies in production.

Critical

MAPE on intermittent or zero-heavy series

What: reporting MAPE on SKUs with zero-demand weeks. Why: it's the default in legacy tools and Excel. Consequence: division by zero → infinite/undefined error; the metric also asymmetrically rewards under-forecasting. Fix: WMAPE for the business view, MASE for cross-series comparison, and judge intermittent items by inventory outcomes. If a stakeholder insists on MAPE, at least exclude zero-actual periods and disclose it.

High

Single-split evaluation (the lucky month)

What: picking the model that won on one hold-out period. Why: it's fast and feels conclusive. Consequence: you select on noise; the "winner" may have been lucky on one unusual month. Fix: rolling-origin CV across ≥4 folds, report mean and spread, and keep a final untouched period for the last gate.

High

Ignoring bias by segment

What: celebrating low overall error while one channel is chronically under-forecast and another over. Why: aggregate bias cancels. Consequence: stock-outs in one segment, overstock in another, even at "good" total accuracy. Fix: report bias sliced by channel, zone, and SKU velocity tier; add a bias-correction step where it's structural.

High

Trees extrapolating a trend they can't see

What: a strongly trending series fed to LightGBM that then flattens out beyond the training range. Why: trees predict within the range of seen targets; they cannot extrapolate. Fix: detrend first (forecast deltas or log-differences), add an explicit time/trend feature, or use a model that extrapolates (ETS/ARIMA/deep) for trended series and keep GBM for the rest.

High

Treating promotions as ordinary demand

What: training on raw sales that include big promo spikes without flagging them. Why: promo calendars are messy and often live in a different system. Consequence: the model bakes a promo into the baseline and over-forecasts the following non-promo weeks. Fix: bring promo/price/display as explicit features (and as future-known covariates), or model baseline and uplift separately.

Medium

No baseline, and over-trusting a single fancy model

What: shipping a complex model without ever comparing to seasonal-naive or a simple ensemble. Why: excitement about the new method. Consequence: you may have spent weeks to do worse than last-year-same-week. Fix: seasonal-naive is mandatory as the floor; combining a couple of decent models (simple average) is one of the most reliable accuracy gains in all of forecasting — the M-competitions have shown this for decades.

Doing it right · 8 min read

Production & lifecycle

A forecast in a notebook is a demo. A forecast that runs every week, survives a SKU launch, a price war, and a festival spike, and tells you when it's degrading — that's a system. Here's what the notebook tutorials leave out.

1 · Ingest & align
Pull sales, on-hand, price, promo calendar, master data. Reconcile calendars (fiscal vs Gregorian), fill structural zeros (no sale ≠ missing), and snap everything to one grain (e.g. SKU × CFA × week). This stage is 60% of the real work.
2 · Feature store / transform
Compute lags, rolling stats, calendar, and future-known covariates (promo/price already committed). Persist features so training and serving use identical logic — training/serving skew is a classic silent bug.
3 · Segment & route
Classify each series (smooth / intermittent / lumpy / new) and route to the right model. One global GBM for the dense majority, intermittent methods for the tail, analogue/foundation-model for cold-start launches.
4 · Train, backtest, select
Rolling-origin CV picks the champion per segment. Log WMAPE/bias/MASE and the chosen model + params to an experiment tracker so every production forecast is reproducible.
5 · Reconcile
Make the SKU→channel→zone→national numbers coherent (MinT/BU). The number the planner stocks to must equal the number leadership commits to.
6 · Serve
Batch is the norm in supply chain — write forecasts + intervals to a table the planning system reads. Expose an on-demand endpoint only for what-if/replanning. Version every forecast run.
7 · Monitor & retrain
Watch realised WMAPE/bias by segment, and input drift (price, mix, new SKUs). Retrain on a schedule and on a drift trigger; alert when accuracy falls below the naive floor.

Drift, retraining, and the cold-start problem

Concept drift — the demand process itself changing (a new competitor, a price reset, a permanent channel shift) — is the reason forecasting is never "done." Calendar-based retraining (weekly/monthly) handles slow drift; a drift trigger (retrain when rolling WMAPE breaches a threshold or input distributions shift) handles abrupt change. Cold-start SKUs have no history to fit: use analogues (a similar SKU's curve), top-down disaggregation from a category forecast, or a foundation model's zero-shot prediction until enough history accrues. Be explicit that new-product forecasts are judgmental-assisted, not statistical, for their first weeks.

State-space, structural & multiple-seasonality models

The classical tier has a deeper layer the foundational view skips: a single state-space framework that unifies ETS and ARIMA, plus the models you actually need when a series has more than one seasonal cycle — which most real demand does.

Underneath ETS and ARIMA sits one idea: a linear Gaussian state-space model. You posit an unobserved state vector αₜ (level, trend, seasonal components) that evolves over time, and you only observe a noisy function of it:

observation:  yₜ = Z αₜ + εₜ    state:  αₜ₊₁ = T αₜ + R ηₜ

The Kalman filter recursively estimates the hidden state: it predicts the next state and its uncertainty, then updates that prediction when the new observation arrives, weighting the two by their relative variances (the Kalman gain). ETS is exactly this in "innovations" form (a single shared error term); ARIMA can be written in state-space form too. This is why both give native prediction intervals — they're tracking the state covariance the whole way. statsmodels' UnobservedComponents exposes the framework directly, letting you compose level + trend + seasonal + cycle + regression blocks.

The Kalman filter loop — predict, observe, update, repeat

The Theta method — the simple model that won M3

Worth knowing because it's a deceptively strong benchmark that beat far more complex methods in the M3 competition. The classic Theta method decomposes the series into "theta lines" (the second differences scaled by a factor θ), forecasts each, and recombines; the standard variant is equivalent to simple exponential smoothing with drift. It costs almost nothing and routinely lands near the top of accuracy tables, so it belongs in your baseline set alongside seasonal-naive and ETS.

Multiple seasonality — the realistic case

Daily retail demand has a weekly cycle and a yearly cycle; intraday data adds a daily cycle. Single-seasonal ETS/ARIMA can't represent that. Four practical tools:

Deep architectures, in depth

The foundational view named N-BEATS, DeepAR, TFT and PatchTST. Here is what actually distinguishes them — the mechanisms you need to choose between them, and the hybrid that won M4.

Every modern neural forecaster is a global model: one network trained across all series, learning shared temporal representations so that a sparse series inherits structure from the population. The theoretical payoff is variance reduction — you trade a little bias (assuming series share structure) for a large drop in estimation variance, which is overwhelmingly worth it when individual series are short. That single idea, not any one architecture, is the reason deep learning became competitive in retail.

DeepAR — autoregressive, probabilistic

An RNN (LSTM/GRU) trained across all series that, rather than predicting a point, parameterises a likelihood (Gaussian, Student-t, or negative-binomial for counts) at each step. You forecast by sampling forward autoregressively many times and reading quantiles off the resulting trajectory distribution. The negative-binomial output is its retail strength — it naturally models over-dispersed count demand. Use it when you want native probabilistic forecasts and have many series sharing dynamics.

N-BEATS & N-HiTS — pure deep stacks

N-BEATS stacks fully-connected blocks with a "doubly residual" design: each block emits a backcast (its reconstruction of the input, subtracted off before the next block) and a forecast (added to the running total). With basis-expansion variants it can separate trend and seasonality interpretably, all without any recurrence or attention. N-HiTS adds multi-rate input sampling and hierarchical interpolation, so different stacks specialise in different frequencies — markedly better and cheaper on long horizons. Both are strong univariate workhorses and excellent when covariates are minimal.

Temporal Fusion Transformer — the covariate specialist

TFT is the architecture to reach for when you have a rich mix of inputs: static covariates (brand, pack size), time-varying known (promo, price, holidays — known into the future) and time-varying unknown (past-only signals). Its distinctive parts: variable selection networks that learn which inputs matter (and expose it), an LSTM encoder-decoder for local processing, and interpretable multi-head attention over time so you can see which past steps drove a forecast. It outputs quantiles directly. The cost is tuning and compute; the payoff is accuracy plus a degree of explainability planners trust.

PatchTST & iTransformer — rethinking the transformer

PatchTST made transformers work for long-horizon forecasting with two moves: slice each series into patches (sub-sequences) as tokens, drastically cutting sequence length, and treat channels independently (channel-independence) rather than mixing variables prematurely. iTransformer inverts the usual setup — it applies attention across variates (each variable is a token) and feed-forward across time, which captures multivariate correlations cleanly. Both top long-horizon multivariate benchmarks; they shine when you have long histories and many related series.

ES-RNN — the hybrid that won M4

The M4 competition winner (Smyl, 2018) was neither pure statistical nor pure deep: it used per-series exponential smoothing to handle level and seasonality (deseasonalising and normalising each series) and a single shared RNN to learn the common non-linear patterns in the residual. This hybrid captures the best of both — local adaptivity from ES, cross-learning from the RNN — and remains the canonical lesson that combining statistical structure with neural flexibility beats either alone. The M5 winner echoed it in spirit with GBM ensembles. Smyl 2020 ↗

Probabilistic forecasting & conformal prediction

Supply-chain decisions consume distributions, not points — yet most teams ship a mean and bolt on a safety factor. This is the rigorous treatment: scoring distributions, calibrating them, and the conformal methods that make a "90% interval" actually mean 90%.

A probabilistic forecast is a predictive distribution F̂ₜ over future demand. You get one three ways: a parametric likelihood (DeepAR's negative-binomial), quantile regression (train at a grid of quantiles with pinball loss — LightGBM, TFT), or sampling (foundation models, ensembles). The question is then how to score a distribution, because WMAPE on the median throws away the entire point.

Proper scoring rules — CRPS and pinball

A scoring rule is proper if it is optimised by the true distribution, so it can't be gamed by under- or over-dispersing. Two you must know:

pinballₙ(y,q) = (y−q)·τ  if y≥q,  else (q−y)·(1−τ)

Pinball (quantile) loss scores a single quantile; averaging it over a dense grid of quantiles approximates the CRPS (Continuous Ranked Probability Score), the standard scalar for a full predictive distribution:

CRPS(F̂, y) = ∫ ( F̂(z) − 𝟙{z ≥ y} )² dz

CRPS generalises MAE — for a point "distribution" it reduces to absolute error — and is reported in the same units as demand, so it's interpretable. For coherent multivariate or hierarchical forecasts, the energy score extends CRPS to vectors. The M5 Uncertainty track scored exactly this family. Always pair the score with calibration (do P90 outcomes really fall below the P90 forecast ~90% of the time? — check via the PIT histogram) and sharpness (are the intervals as narrow as honesty allows?). The goal is the sharpest calibrated forecast — not the narrowest, and not the most "accurate" mean.

Conformal prediction — coverage you can trust

Model-native intervals are routinely over-confident. Conformal prediction wraps any forecaster and produces intervals with a finite-sample coverage guarantee from a calibration set — but its classical guarantee rests on exchangeability, which time series violate through temporal dependence and distribution shift. The 2021–2026 literature is essentially the project of restoring coverage despite that. Four families: conformal TS review 2025 ↗

# CRPS + conformalized quantile intervals on top of any base forecaster import numpy as np def crps_from_quantiles(y, qpreds, levels): # average pinball over a quantile grid approximates CRPS losses = [] for q, tau in zip(qpreds, levels): e = y - q losses.append(np.maximum(tau*e, (tau-1)*e)) return 2 * np.mean(losses) # CQR: calibrate quantile-regression intervals to hit nominal coverage def cqr_calibrate(lo_cal, hi_cal, y_cal, alpha=0.1): scores = np.maximum(lo_cal - y_cal, y_cal - hi_cal) # nonconformity n = len(y_cal) qhat = np.quantile(scores, np.ceil((n+1)*(1-alpha))/n, method="higher") return qhat # widen test intervals by qhat: [lo - qhat, hi + qhat]

Hierarchical probabilistic reconciliation is the frontier where this meets the hierarchy section: methods like bootstrap reconciliation and PERMBU reconcile whole distributions (not just point forecasts) so that coherent intervals add up — important when leaves are intermittent and the sum behaves differently from the parts. Advanced evaluation — significance & forecastability

"Model B's WMAPE is 0.31 vs 0.33" is not a result until you know the difference is real and the series was forecastable in the first place. Two disciplines most teams skip.

Is the difference significant? Diebold-Mariano

The Diebold-Mariano (DM) test asks whether two forecasts have equal expected accuracy, accounting for the fact that forecast errors are autocorrelated and non-Gaussian. You form the loss differential dₜ = L(e₁ₜ) − L(e₂ₜ) for any loss L (absolute, squared, pinball, even CRPS), and test whether its mean is zero:

DM = d̄ / √( V̂ar(d̄) )  ~ N(0,1) under H₀: E[dₜ] = 0

The variance uses an autocorrelation-robust (long-run) estimator; for multi-step or small samples use the Harvey-Leybourne-Newbold correction. The practical payoff: it stops you from promoting a "winner" whose edge is within noise — the same discipline as the hysteresis in Project 5's selector ↗. dm.test reference ↗

When you compare many models across many series (the M-competition setting), pairwise DM tests don't scale; use MCB (Multiple Comparisons with the Best) / the Nemenyi test, which ranks methods per series and draws a critical-difference diagram showing which models are statistically indistinguishable from the best. This is the standard the M4/M5 papers used to declare winners.

# Diebold-Mariano on two models' errors (HLN small-sample correction) import numpy as np from scipy import stats def diebold_mariano(e1, e2, h=1, power=1): d = np.abs(e1)**power - np.abs(e2)**power n = len(d); dbar = d.mean() # long-run variance with Newey-West style truncation at h-1 lags gamma0 = np.var(d, ddof=0) acov = sum((1 - k/h) * np.cov(d[k:], d[:-k])[0,1] for k in range(1, h)) if h>1 else 0 var = (gamma0 + 2*acov) / n dm = dbar / np.sqrt(var) hln = np.sqrt((n + 1 - 2*h + h*(h-1)/n) / n) * dm # Harvey-Leybourne-Newbold p = 2 * (1 - stats.t.cdf(np.abs(hln), df=n-1)) return hln, p # p < 0.05 -> accuracy difference is significant

Could this series even be forecast?

Some series are intrinsically unforecastable — pure noise around a mean — and no model will beat naive on them; chasing accuracy there is wasted effort. Measure forecastability before modelling: the coefficient of variation and ADI (per the intermittent section) for demand pattern, and spectral entropy — the entropy of the series' power spectrum, near 1 for white noise and near 0 for a highly predictable (concentrated-spectrum) series. Routing low-forecastability SKUs to a robust naive/Croston method and spending model budget on the forecastable ones is one of the highest-leverage operational decisions you can make.

Backtest design traps that survive even rolling-origin CV: (1) a gap is needed between train and test when features use future-relative windows, or you leak; (2) anchored (expanding) vs sliding windows answer different questions — sliding tests adaptivity to drift, anchored tests use-all-history; (3) tuning on the test folds (choosing hyperparameters by peeking at the same folds you report) silently overfits — nest a validation split inside each fold.

From forecast to decision — inventory & the newsvendor

The forecast is never the goal; the order quantity is. This is the link the purely-statistical treatments omit — and it changes which forecast you should even produce.

The single most important idea connecting forecasting to supply chain is the newsvendor model. If under-stocking costs you C℅ per unit (lost margin, lost sales) and over-stocking costs Cₒ per unit (holding, markdown, spoilage), the cost-optimal order quantity is not the mean forecast — it's the quantile of the demand distribution at the critical fractile:

Q* = F⁻¹( C℅ / (C℅ + Cₒ) )    ⇒   order the critical-fractile quantile of demand

Read that carefully, because it reframes everything: the service level you target is the critical fractile, and the number you should forecast is that quantile, not the mean. A high-margin snack where stock-outs are expensive (C℅ ≫ Cₒ) has a critical fractile near 0.95 — you deliberately over-order to the P95. A perishable with steep markdown (Cₒ high) has a low fractile — you under-order to, say, the P60. This is precisely why the probabilistic forecasting section matters operationally: pinball loss at quantile τ is the same asymmetric loss as the newsvendor cost with critical fractile τ. Optimising the right quantile loss is optimising expected inventory cost.

Critical fractile sets the order quantile — not the mean

For replenishment over a lead time L, you need the lead-time demand distribution — the sum of demand over L periods — not single-period demand. With a normal approximation, safety stock = zSL · σₗₜ₌ where σₗₜ₌ is the standard deviation of lead-time demand and zSL the z-score of the service level; but a quantile/probabilistic forecast of cumulative lead-time demand is more honest, especially for skewed or intermittent items where normality fails badly.

# Newsvendor order quantity straight from a probabilistic forecast import numpy as np def newsvendor_order(samples_leadtime_demand, cu, co): """samples: array of simulated lead-time demand from the forecast.""" critical_fractile = cu / (cu + co) return np.quantile(samples_leadtime_demand, critical_fractile) # e.g. high-margin SKU: understock cost 8/unit, overstock cost 1.5/unit Q = newsvendor_order(lead_time_samples, cu=8.0, co=1.5) # ~ P84 of demand
Forecast-then-optimise vs decision-focused learning. The classic pipeline forecasts demand, then optimises inventory separately. A growing line of work trains the model to minimise the downstream decision cost directly (decision-focused / end-to-end learning), so the forecast is tuned to make good orders rather than low WMAPE. For now, the pragmatic 80/20 is: forecast the right quantile for each SKU's cost ratio, and judge the system on inventory KPIs (fill rate, holding cost), not point accuracy.
Advanced · 10 min read

Special demand problems

The cases that break the standard pipeline: products with no history, promotions that distort it, and sales data that lies because of stock-outs. Each needs its own machinery.

New-product introduction (NPI) — forecasting with no history

You can't fit a model to data that doesn't exist, so NPI forecasting borrows structure. The classic parametric tool is the Bass diffusion model, which describes adoption of a new product as an S-curve driven by innovators (coefficient p, external influence like advertising) and imitators (coefficient q, word-of-mouth), scaling to market potential m:

f(t)/(1−F(t)) = p + q·F(t)  ⇒  sales(t) = m·[ (p+q)² / p ]·e^(−(p+q)t) / (1 + (q/p)e^(−(p+q)t))²

You estimate p, q, m from analogues (similar past launches) until real sales accrue. Complementary practical methods: analogue forecasting (clone a similar SKU's launch curve and scale), attribute-based models (regress first-year demand on pack size, price tier, brand, channel), and structured judgment (sales input with bias tracking). The system should blend analogue → fitted model over the first 8–12 weeks so planners see no discontinuity — exactly the cold-start design in Project 5 ↗.

Promotions, price & the baseline-uplift split

Promotions are the dominant swing factor in FMCG and the easiest way to wreck a model that treats them as ordinary demand. The robust pattern is baseline + uplift: forecast the non-promo baseline, then separately model the multiplicative uplift as a function of mechanic, depth, display, and feature. Estimate price elasticity with a log-log specification (the coefficient on log-price is the elasticity directly):

log(demand) = α + β·log(price) + γ·promo + δ·display + seasonality + ε  (β = price elasticity)

Two effects planners must not ignore: cannibalisation (promoting SKU A steals demand from substitute B) and halo (it lifts complements), plus pull-forward (a promo borrows from future non-promo weeks, leaving a dip). Capture these by including cross-SKU promo features and a post-promo indicator, or you'll over-forecast the weeks after every event. Crucially, treat committed future promos/prices as future-known covariates so the model responds to planned actions, not just correlations.

Predictive ≠ causal. A model that fits "promo weeks sell more" can't tell you the incremental effect of a new promo if promos were historically run when demand was already rising (confounding). For genuine what-if and elasticity, you need a causal design (controlled tests, or methods like double machine learning), not just a high-R² predictive fit.

Censored demand & unconstraining

Your data records sales, but you want to model demand. When an item stocks out, observed sales are censored — capped at available supply — so true demand was higher than recorded. Train naively and the model learns availability, perpetually under-stocking items that stock out (a vicious cycle). The fix is demand unconstraining: flag stock-out periods, treat their sales as a lower bound rather than the truth, and recover the latent demand — classically via Expectation-Maximisation (estimate the demand distribution, impute the censored periods' expected uncensored value, refit), or by modelling with survival/Tobit-style censored likelihoods. Even a simple rule — drop or impute known stock-out weeks before fitting — beats ignoring it. This is named as an open problem in the frontier section, but the EM unconstraining workflow is production-ready today.

Frontier · 10 min read

The 2026 frontier

Where the field is actively moving right now: a fast-consolidating foundation-model landscape, reconciliation across both space and time, demand sensing, and the shift from predicting demand to optimising the decision.

The foundation-model landscape, late-2025 / 2026

The space consolidated fast around a shared benchmark and a second generation of models. GIFT-Eval (Salesforce, late 2024) is now the de-facto general benchmark — 97 task configurations across 55 datasets, with a public leaderboard — so claims are finally comparable. GIFT-Eval ↗ The current field includes Chronos-2 (unifies univariate, multivariate and covariate-informed forecasting via in-context group attention — directly fixing gen-1's covariate weakness), Moirai-2.0 (decoder-only with a quantile head) and Moirai-MoE (mixture-of-experts for per-frequency specialisation), TimesFM-2.0/2.5, plus Toto (Datadog), Sundial (flow-matching, no tokenisation), TabPFN-TS, and efficiency-focused TinyTimeMixer (TTM) and Time-MoE.

Two honest caveats the marketing omits. First, benchmark leakage: several models were pretrained on corpora overlapping common test sets, and studies show their apparent edge collapses on genuinely unseen data (large MSE gains on leaked datasets shrink to low-single-digit percentages on non-leaked ones). TSFM leakage study ↗ Second, calibration: zero-shot probabilistic outputs are not always well-calibrated, so verify coverage with the methods in Probabilistic forecasting before trusting their intervals. The 2026 stance is unchanged in spirit but sharper: foundation models are an excellent, near-instant baseline, cold-start tool, and ensemble member — and for covariate-rich retail, a tuned GBM (or Chronos-2 / ChronosX with covariates, fine-tuned) is still where you end up.

Temporal & cross-temporal reconciliation

The hierarchy section reconciled across structure (SKU→zone→national). You can also reconcile across time: forecast the same series at weekly, monthly, and quarterly granularity and make those agree — temporal hierarchies (THieF). Aggregating to coarser frequencies suppresses noise and stabilises long-horizon forecasts, while the fine level keeps operational detail; reconciling them borrows strength in both directions and is often a free accuracy gain. The current frontier, cross-temporal reconciliation, does both at once — coherent across the product/geography hierarchy and across time frequencies simultaneously — which is exactly the structure an S&OP process implicitly demands (operational weekly numbers that tie to the monthly financial plan that ties to the annual budget).

Demand sensing & external signals

Demand sensing is the short-horizon complement to demand planning: it uses high-frequency, near-real-time signals — POS/sell-out, downstream inventory, weather, web traffic, search trends, mobility — to adjust the next few days/weeks, capturing shifts before they appear in shipment history. It doesn't replace the statistical/ML forecast; it nowcasts a correction on top of it. The practical challenges are signal latency and noise (many external signals are themselves forecasts or arrive late), and proving incremental value — measure demand-sensing lift with the same forecast-value-added discipline you apply to human overrides, or it becomes expensive theatre.

Causal & decision-focused forecasting; LLM agents

  • Causal forecasting — separating correlation from the incremental effect of planned interventions (a new promo, a price change) so the forecast responds correctly to decisions, not just history. Methods: controlled tests, double machine learning, causal-impact / synthetic-control for measuring past events.
  • Decision-focused / end-to-end learning — training the model to minimise downstream inventory cost rather than forecast error, so it optimises the order, not the WMAPE (see From forecast to decision).
  • Censored-demand & intermittent-hierarchical reconciliation — recovering true demand from stock-out-censored sales, and reconciling coherent distributions when leaves are intermittent, remain genuinely open.
  • LLM / agentic forecasting — using language models to orchestrate the pipeline (feature selection, model choice, anomaly explanation, narrative S&OP commentary) and to fuse unstructured signals (news, supplier emails) with numeric series. Early, hype-prone, but a real 2026 research thread.

Practice · interview & self-check

Q&A bank

Eighty questions from "explain it in 60 seconds" to system design, each answer self-contained enough to learn from. Filter by category; difficulty is tagged on every item.

What is demand forecasting, in one minute?
Beginner▾

It's predicting future demand for products — how many units sell, where, and when — to drive replenishment, production, and planning decisions. Properly understood, the output is a probability distribution over future quantities, not a single number; we usually summarise it as a point forecast plus a prediction interval. The craft is choosing the right level (SKU × location × period), the right method for the data's shape, and the right error metric, then proving the model beats a naive baseline on honest backtesting.

What's the difference between a point forecast and a probabilistic forecast?
Beginner▾

A point forecast is a single expected value (mean or median). A probabilistic forecast describes the whole distribution — typically as quantiles or a prediction interval. Inventory decisions consume the distribution: safety stock for a 95% service level needs the P95, not the mean. Serving only the mean means you'll be short roughly half the time, so probabilistic forecasting is usually what supply chain actually needs.

Why is seasonal-naive such an important model if it's so simple?
Beginner▾

Because it's the floor every real model must clear. Seasonal-naive forecasts each future period as the same period one season ago (e.g. this week = same week last year). It's free, robust, and shockingly hard to beat on many series. If your sophisticated model can't beat seasonal-naive on rolling-origin backtesting, it isn't adding value — and a surprising number of deployed models quietly fail this test. It's also the denominator of MASE, so it doubles as a comparison yardstick.

What does it mean to decompose a time series?
Beginner▾

Splitting the series into interpretable parts: trend (slow drift), seasonality (repeating cycles), often calendar/event effects (holidays, promos), and a remainder (noise). Decomposition can be additive (y = trend + season + remainder) or multiplicative (y = trend × season × remainder), the latter when seasonal swings scale with the level. It's both a diagnostic (what's driving this series?) and the conceptual basis for classical models like ETS.

When should I use a classical model instead of machine learning?
Beginner▾

When you have a single (or few) clean series with clear, stable seasonality and a few hundred observations, and you don't need to model covariates like price/promo. ETS and AutoARIMA are fast, interpretable, hard to overfit, and give native prediction intervals. ML earns its keep when you have many series (cross-learning), rich covariates, and scale — i.e. typical retail. A good practice is to always run a classical model as one of your baselines regardless.

What is cross-learning (a global model)?
Intermediate▾

Training one model across many time series at once, instead of fitting a separate model per series. The model learns patterns shared across series, so a sparse SKU borrows strength from its category and you maintain one model instead of millions. It was the decisive idea in the M5 competition and underpins both gradient-boosting and deep-learning approaches to retail forecasting. The trade-off is that a global model assumes the series are related enough to share structure — usually true within a catalogue.

Why did LightGBM dominate the M5 retail competition?
Intermediate▾

Practically all of the top 50 M5 teams used LightGBM. It fits retail data well: it ingests rich features (price, promo, calendar, lags), captures non-linear interactions, trains fast, and supports cross-learning across series. The winner used an equal-weighted ensemble of LightGBM models trained at different aggregation levels (store, store-category, store-department), averaging recursive and direct variants — 220 models in total. The lesson wasn't "one magic model" but "tree ensembles + cross-learning + combining."

What's the difference between ETS and ARIMA?
Intermediate▾

Both are classical, but they model structure differently. ETS (exponential smoothing) describes the series through evolving components — level, trend, seasonal — updated by weighted averages that decay into the past. ARIMA models the series through its own lagged values (AR) and lagged forecast errors (MA) after differencing (I) to achieve stationarity. In practice they often perform similarly; ETS is more intuitive for trend/seasonal series, ARIMA more flexible for autocorrelation structure. Modern tooling auto-selects either's parameters by information criteria.

What are foundation models for time series?
Intermediate▾

Large models pretrained on huge, diverse collections of time series that can forecast a brand-new series with no training (zero-shot), analogous to how an LLM answers about unseen topics. Examples shipping open weights by 2026 include TimesFM (Google), Chronos (Amazon), Moirai (Salesforce), and Lag-Llama; TimeGPT (Nixtla) is API-hosted. They give fast deployment and strong cold-start behaviour, but on covariate-rich retail data a well-tuned GBM often still beats them, so they're best used as a strong baseline and ensemble member.

What is the forecast horizon, and why does it matter?
Beginner▾

The horizon is how far ahead you forecast (e.g. 8 weeks). It matters because accuracy degrades with horizon, the right model can differ by horizon, and the horizon must match the decision's lead time — there's no point forecasting 1 week ahead if replenishment takes 4. Always report accuracy by horizon, not just an average, because a model strong at h=1 can be weak at h=8.

What is a covariate (exogenous variable) in forecasting?
Beginner▾

An external driver beyond the series' own history — price, promo flag, holiday, weather, listing status. Covariates split into future-known (you'll know next week's promo plan in advance) and future-unknown (next week's weather is itself a forecast). Models differ in how well they use them: ARIMAX and especially gradient boosting and TFT handle covariates well, while pure zero-shot foundation models struggle to. Using committed promo/price as future-known covariates is one of the biggest accuracy levers in retail.

What does "stationary" mean and why do some models require it?
Intermediate▾

A series is (weakly) stationary if its statistical properties — mean, variance, autocorrelation — don't change over time. ARIMA's AR/MA machinery assumes stationarity, which is why it differences the series (the "I") to remove trend and uses seasonal differencing for seasonality. Tree and deep models don't formally require it, but trends still cause problems (trees can't extrapolate), so detrending or trend features help there too.

Bottom-up, top-down, middle-out — what's the difference?
Intermediate▾

They're ways to make a forecast hierarchy coherent. Bottom-up forecasts the leaves and sums upward (unbiased, but noisy at the top). Top-down forecasts the total and splits it down by proportions (stable at top, biased and weak at the bottom). Middle-out forecasts a reliable middle level, going bottom-up above and top-down below. MinT (optimal reconciliation) uses all levels at once and is usually the most accurate while staying coherent.

What libraries should I actually use in 2026?
Beginner▾

For classical at scale: Nixtla's statsforecast (AutoARIMA/AutoETS/Croston/TSB). For ML: lightgbm with mlforecast for feature/lag handling. For deep models: neuralforecast or Darts (N-HiTS, TFT, PatchTST). For hierarchy: hierarchicalforecast (BU/TD/MinT). For foundation models: Chronos, TimesFM, Moirai/uni2ts, Lag-Llama, or TimeGPT's API. Darts and sktime are good "batteries-included" front-ends; statsmodels and Prophet remain common entry points.

My backtest looks great but live accuracy is terrible. What happened?
Intermediate▾

Almost always data leakage: a feature used information that wouldn't be available at forecast time. Common culprits are rolling statistics that include the target period, target encodings computed over the full dataset, or covariates joined on the wrong date. The fix is strict cutoff discipline — every lag/rolling feature uses .shift() so it only sees the past, and features are computed inside each CV fold. A secondary cause is that backtests assume perfect future knowledge of promo/price and clean data, which live runs don't have; expect a few points of degradation and instrument for it.

Why does MAPE blow up on some of my SKUs?
Beginner▾

Because MAPE divides by the actual, and those SKUs have zero-demand periods — division by zero gives infinite or undefined values, and near-zero actuals give huge percentages. MAPE is also asymmetric: it penalises over-forecasting less than under-forecasting, biasing model selection. On intermittent or zero-heavy data, switch to WMAPE (weighted by total volume, survives zeros) or MASE (scaled against seasonal-naive). Reserve MAPE for strictly-positive, smooth series.

My GBM under-forecasts a clearly rising series. Why?
Intermediate▾

Tree models can't extrapolate. A tree's prediction is an average of training targets that fall in a leaf, so it can never output a value higher than what it saw in training. On a trending series, once you forecast beyond the historical range, the tree flattens out and under-forecasts. Fixes: detrend first (model differences or log-differences and re-integrate), add an explicit time/trend feature, or use a model that extrapolates (ETS/ARIMA/deep) for trended series and reserve trees for the de-trended remainder.

My overall bias is near zero but I still get stock-outs. How?
Advanced▾

Aggregate bias cancels. You can be +15% on one channel and −15% on another and report ~0% overall, while the under-forecast segment runs dry and the over-forecast one overstocks. Always slice bias by channel, zone, and velocity tier. Even within a series, a forecast that's right on average but too low in peak weeks causes stock-outs precisely when they hurt — which is why you also forecast to a service-level quantile rather than the mean for items where availability matters.

Croston keeps forecasting demand for an item that died. Why won't it go to zero?
Intermediate▾

Because Croston (and SBA) only update their estimates in periods with positive demand. During a long run of zeros — exactly what obsolescence looks like — there's nothing to trigger an update, so the forecast stays frozen at its last positive-demand value. The fix is TSB (Teunter-Syntetos-Babai), which tracks demand probability and updates it every period, including zeros, so the forecast decays toward zero for a dying SKU. Use TSB whenever obsolescence is a real risk.

Is Croston biased? What does SBA actually fix?
Advanced▾

Yes — Croston is positively biased: it systematically over-estimates the demand rate because of how the ratio of smoothed size to smoothed interval behaves in expectation. The Syntetos-Boylan Approximation multiplies the Croston forecast by (1 − α/2), where α is the size smoothing constant, removing most of the bias. In practice SBA gives lower bias and is the usual default over vanilla Croston for intermittent (non-obsolescent) items; for items that can go obsolete, TSB is preferred.

I picked the model that won last month and it's now worse. What went wrong?
Intermediate▾

You selected on a single split, which is mostly noise. One month can be a holiday, a stock-out, or a launch — unrepresentative. A model that "won" there may have been lucky. Use rolling-origin cross-validation across several folds and choose on the average and the spread; a model that's consistently second can be a better production choice than one that's volatile but occasionally first. Keep a final untouched period as the last gate.

Why are my model's 90% prediction intervals only right 60% of the time?
Advanced▾

Model-native intervals are often over-confident — they assume the model is correctly specified and ignore parameter and structural uncertainty. The result is intervals that are too narrow, so realised coverage is below nominal. The modern fix is conformal prediction: a distribution-free wrapper that calibrates intervals on held-out data so a "90%" interval actually achieves ~90% coverage under an exchangeability assumption. Always validate interval coverage empirically; don't trust nominal levels.

A big promo week wrecks my forecasts for the following weeks. Why?
Intermediate▾

If promotions aren't flagged as features, the model treats the promo spike as ordinary demand and bakes it into the baseline or recent lags, then over-forecasts the normal weeks that follow (and it may also miss the pull-forward dip after a promo). The fix is to bring promo, price, and display as explicit features — ideally as future-known covariates so the model knows when the next promo is — or to model baseline demand and promo uplift separately and recombine.

My sales data shows zero — was demand actually zero?
Advanced▾

Not necessarily — this is censored demand. A zero can mean "no one wanted it" or "we were out of stock, so demand couldn't be observed." If you train on sales as if they were demand, the model learns availability, not demand, and will keep under-stocking items that stock out. Mitigations: flag/known stock-out periods and treat them as missing rather than zero, impute unconstrained demand, or use models that account for censoring. It's an active research area and a frequent source of silent bias.

Why does my recursive multi-step forecast get worse the further out it goes — more than I'd expect?
Advanced▾

Recursive forecasting feeds each prediction back in as the lag for the next step, so errors compound: a small miss at h=1 corrupts the lag used at h=2, and so on. The degradation is faster than the inherent difficulty of the horizon. The alternative is direct forecasting — a separate model per horizon that only uses lags old enough to be known at that horizon — which avoids feedback but multiplies model count. M5 winners averaged recursive and direct to get the best of both.

Does a higher R² mean a better forecast?
Beginner▾

No. R² measures in-sample fit, not out-of-sample forecast accuracy, and you can drive it arbitrarily high by overfitting. Forecasting is judged on held-out future periods with proper metrics (WMAPE, MASE, bias) under rolling-origin backtesting. A model can have a beautiful R² on history and lose to seasonal-naive on the future. Ignore in-sample fit statistics for forecast selection.

I added more features and the forecast got worse. How?
Intermediate▾

More features means more chances to overfit noise and more chances to leak the future. A feature that's predictive in-sample but unstable, or that subtly encodes the target, hurts out-of-sample accuracy. Validate every feature addition on rolling-origin CV, not in-sample fit, prune low-importance and unstable features, and audit each for leakage. Parsimony often wins, especially for the long tail of sparse series.

Should I always reach for a deep learning model since they're state of the art?
Intermediate▾

No. On typical tabular retail panels, a well-built gradient-boosting model frequently matches or beats deep models at a fraction of the compute and tuning cost — this is exactly what the M5 results showed. Deep models earn their complexity with many long, covariate-rich series and abundant data. Always benchmark honestly against a GBM and classical baselines on rolling-origin CV before committing to the operational burden of a deep model.

Why is my "unbiased" bottom-up forecast worse at the national level than forecasting national directly?
Advanced▾

Bottom-up sums many noisy leaf forecasts, and the errors, while individually unbiased, accumulate — the top-level total inherits all the leaf variance. A direct national forecast averages out a lot of that noise. This is the classic tension reconciliation addresses: MinT uses information from all levels (including the more stable top) to produce a coherent set that's typically more accurate at every level than pure bottom-up or top-down.

How often should I retrain a demand forecasting model?
Intermediate▾

Use both a schedule and a trigger. Calendar retraining (weekly or monthly, matching your planning cadence) handles slow drift and new data. A drift trigger — retrain when rolling realised WMAPE breaches a threshold or falls below the seasonal-naive floor, or when input distributions shift — handles abrupt change like a price reset or a new competitor. Avoid retraining so often you chase noise; log metrics each cycle and only promote a challenger that beats the champion on backtesting.

What's the difference between training/serving skew and concept drift?
Advanced▾

Training/serving skew is an engineering bug: features computed differently at training vs serving time (different code, different data freshness), so the model sees inconsistent inputs. Concept drift is a real-world change: the demand process itself shifts (new entrant, price regime, mix change), so a once-good model degrades. Skew is fixed by sharing feature logic (a feature store); drift is handled by monitoring and retraining. They look similar in symptoms (live accuracy drops) but have completely different fixes.

How do I forecast a brand-new product with no history?
Advanced▾

Cold-start has no series to fit, so you borrow structure: use analogues (the launch curve of a similar SKU), top-down disaggregation from a category forecast, attribute-based models (predict from pack size, price tier, brand), or a foundation model's zero-shot prediction. Be explicit that these are judgmental-assisted, not statistical, and design a smooth hand-off to a fitted model as history accrues (e.g. blend weights that shift from analogue to model over the first 8–12 weeks) so planners don't see a discontinuity.

Batch or real-time serving for demand forecasts?
Intermediate▾

Batch is the norm in supply chain: forecasts are generated on a schedule (nightly/weekly) and written to a table the planning system reads, because replenishment decisions run on a cadence, not per-request. Expose an on-demand endpoint only for interactive what-if/replanning (a planner testing a promo scenario). Version every batch run so any forecast can be reproduced and audited. Real-time inference is rarely needed and adds operational complexity for little benefit here.

What should I monitor in production?
Intermediate▾

Three layers: (1) accuracy — realised WMAPE, bias, and MASE by segment (channel, zone, velocity tier), with an alert if any segment falls below the seasonal-naive floor; (2) input/data quality — missing feeds, late or revised data, schema drift, sudden distribution shifts in price/mix; (3) operational — job success, latency, forecast coverage (did every active SKU get a forecast?). Bias by segment is the highest-signal accuracy metric because it predicts stock-outs and overstock before they show up as cost.

How do I handle the long tail of millions of sparse SKUs efficiently?
Advanced▾

Segment and route. Classify each series by ADI/CV²; send the dense majority to one cross-learned global model (fast, shares strength), the intermittent tail to lightweight Croston/SBA/TSB (cheap, no per-series tuning), and cold-start items to analogues/foundation models. Use a library built for scale (Nixtla's statsforecast/mlforecast run millions of series; distribute with Spark/Ray if needed). The point is to spend compute where signal exists and use robust cheap methods where it doesn't.

Why is live accuracy always a bit worse than backtest, even with no leakage?
Intermediate▾

Backtests are run on cleaned, fully-revised history and usually assume you knew future promo/price perfectly. Live runs face late, dirty, revised data, imperfect forward promo plans, and stock-out censoring. So even a leakage-free backtest is mildly optimistic. The healthy response is to expect a few points of degradation, measure the backtest-to-live gap explicitly, and treat a widening gap as a monitoring signal rather than a surprise.

How do I reconcile a statistical forecast with sales/planner overrides?
Advanced▾

Treat overrides as a managed layer, not free edits. Capture the statistical baseline and the human-adjusted final separately so you can measure forecast value-added (FVA) — whether overrides actually improve accuracy (often they don't). Require a reason code for large overrides, monitor override bias by person/segment, and feed systematic, justified adjustments (a known future event) back as features rather than perpetual manual tweaks. The goal is to keep human judgment where it adds information and remove it where it just adds noise.

How do I make a forecast run reproducible and auditable?
Intermediate▾

Version everything: the input data snapshot, the feature logic, the model and its hyperparameters, and the output forecast, all tied to a run ID. Log the backtest metrics that justified the chosen model to an experiment tracker. Then any forecast in the planning system can be traced to exactly the data and code that produced it — essential for debugging a bad cycle and for the governance questions leadership will eventually ask.

Should I use a foundation model in production today?
Advanced▾

As a baseline, ensemble member, and cold-start fallback — yes, that's low-risk and useful. As your sole production model on covariate-rich retail data — usually not yet, because a tuned GBM typically wins and injecting promo/price into a zero-shot model is awkward. Weigh inference cost, latency, and governance for large models. The pragmatic 2026 pattern is hybrid: GBM for the dense covariate-driven majority, foundation model for cold-start and as a challenger you keep measuring against.

What is forecast value added (FVA)?
Intermediate▾

FVA measures whether each step in your forecasting process actually improves accuracy over the naive baseline. You compare the error of the statistical forecast, the human-adjusted forecast, and seasonal-naive on the same periods. If the statistical model beats naive, it adds value; if planner overrides make it worse, that step has negative FVA and should be curtailed. It's a discipline for cutting effort that doesn't help — a surprising amount of manual touching reduces accuracy.

How do I keep training and serving features identical?
Advanced▾

Share one code path. Define feature transformations once and call the same function in training and serving (or use a feature store that materialises features for both). Pin library versions, snapshot the feature definitions with the model, and add a test that recomputes a sample of serving features and compares them to the training pipeline's output. Training/serving skew is a silent killer — the model looks fine in backtest and quietly underperforms live because the inputs differ.

How do I integrate forecasts with inventory/replenishment decisions?
Advanced▾

Forecasts feed the inventory policy, they aren't the decision themselves. Replenishment needs lead-time demand and a service-level target, which is why you forecast the relevant quantile (or a full distribution) over the lead time, not just the mean. Safety stock derives from the forecast's uncertainty; order-up-to levels combine the lead-time demand forecast with that buffer. The implication: forecast quality should be judged partly by the inventory outcome (fill rate, holding cost), not point accuracy alone.

Derive what the smoothing parameter α controls in exponential smoothing.
Intermediate▾

Simple exponential smoothing is ŷₜ₊₁ = αyₜ + (1−α)ŷₜ. Unrolling the recursion shows the forecast is a weighted sum of all past observations with geometrically decaying weights: α, α(1−α), α(1−α)², … So α sets the memory. A high α (→1) weights recent data heavily — responsive but jumpy; a low α (→0) averages over a long past — smooth but slow to react. You choose α by minimising in-sample one-step error (or let AutoETS optimise it), trading reactivity against stability.

Write out WMAPE and explain why it's preferred over MAPE for retail.
Intermediate▾

WMAPE = Σ|actualₜ − forecastₜ| / Σ|actualₜ|, i.e. total absolute error divided by total actual demand. Unlike MAPE (which averages per-period percentage errors and divides each by that period's actual), WMAPE divides by the sum, so individual zeros don't blow it up and high-volume periods dominate appropriately. That makes it well-defined on intermittent data and aligned with the business question "what fraction of total volume did we miss?" It's essentially a volume-weighted absolute percentage error.

What exactly does MASE scale by, and what does MASE = 1 mean?
Advanced▾

MASE = MAE of your forecast ÷ MAE of the in-sample one-step seasonal-naive forecast. The denominator is the average absolute change between observations one season apart in the training data, which puts every series on a common, scale-free footing. MASE = 1 means you're exactly as accurate as seasonal-naive; below 1 means you beat it, above 1 means worse. Because the denominator is in-sample, MASE can be unstable when that naive error is tiny, so guard against near-zero denominators.

How does the ADI/CV² classification get its cutoffs?
Advanced▾

Syntetos and Boylan derived the cutoffs (ADI ≈ 1.32, CV² ≈ 0.49) from the theoretical point at which one estimator's mean squared error overtakes another's — i.e. where SBA starts beating Croston and where the demand pattern crosses from "smooth" to "intermittent/erratic/lumpy." ADI (average inter-demand interval) measures how often demand occurs; CV² (squared coefficient of variation of non-zero sizes) measures how variable the sizes are. The four quadrants then map to recommended methods.

What is MinT minimising, and why can it beat bottom-up?
Expert▾

MinT (Minimum Trace) finds the reconciled forecast that minimises the trace of the covariance matrix of the reconciled forecast errors — i.e. the total variance of errors across all levels — subject to coherence. Mechanically it projects the base forecasts onto the coherent subspace using a weighting derived from the base forecast-error covariance. Because it uses information from every level (including stable aggregate levels) rather than only the leaves, it can reduce error everywhere; when the base forecasts are unbiased it provably does no worse than bottom-up. The practical challenge is estimating that covariance well, hence shrinkage variants.

What does the Tweedie objective do for unit-sales models?
Advanced▾

Unit sales are non-negative, zero-inflated, and right-skewed — many zeros plus a long tail of positive values. The Tweedie distribution (with variance power between 1 and 2) is a compound Poisson-Gamma that models exactly this: a point mass at zero plus a skewed positive part. Using a Tweedie objective in LightGBM lets the model fit zero-heavy demand directly rather than forcing a Gaussian assumption that mishandles the zeros. The variance power (e.g. ~1.1–1.3) tunes how much mass sits at zero.

Explain pinball (quantile) loss and what minimising it gives you.
Advanced▾

Pinball loss for quantile q penalises under-prediction by q·(error) and over-prediction by (1−q)·(error), an asymmetric absolute loss. Minimising the q-pinball loss yields the conditional q-quantile of the target. So training with pinball loss at q=0.9 produces a forecast that the truth should exceed only 10% of the time — exactly what you want for a 90% service level. Averaging pinball loss over several quantiles (a multi-quantile loss) trains a full predictive distribution, and it's the metric the M5 Uncertainty track used.

Why does differencing make ARIMA work, and what does over-differencing cost?
Advanced▾

Differencing (yₜ − yₜ₋₁) removes trend and helps achieve stationarity, which ARIMA's AR/MA structure assumes. Seasonal differencing removes seasonal level shifts. But over-differencing injects artificial negative autocorrelation and inflates variance, producing noisier forecasts and unnecessary MA terms. The order d is chosen by unit-root tests and information criteria; AutoARIMA searches this so you rarely set it by hand. The principle: difference just enough to be stationary, no more.

What's the bias-variance intuition for choosing global vs per-series models?
Expert▾

A per-series model has low bias for that series but high variance, because it estimates many parameters from little data — disastrous for sparse SKUs. A global (cross-learned) model pools data across series, slashing variance at the cost of some bias from assuming shared structure. For short or sparse series the variance reduction dominates and the global model wins handily; for a few long, idiosyncratic series a dedicated model can be better. This is why retail, dominated by many short series, favours global models.

Why is RMSE dominated by spikes while WMAPE/MAE aren't?
Intermediate▾

RMSE squares each error before averaging, so a single large miss contributes its error squared — one 10× outlier dominates many small errors. MAE and WMAPE use absolute errors, which grow linearly, so they're far more robust to spikes. Choose RMSE when large errors are disproportionately costly (you want the model to fear them); choose MAE/WMAPE when you want a typical-error view that promo spikes won't hijack. Optimising RMSE also pulls forecasts toward the mean, while MAE pulls toward the median.

In TSB, why update demand probability every period instead of the interval?
Expert▾

Croston's interval is only updated when demand occurs, so during zero runs the estimate is frozen — fine for stable intermittency, fatal for obsolescence. TSB reframes the occurrence as a probability p updated by exponential smoothing every period: on a zero period p is smoothed toward 0, on a demand period toward 1. The forecast is p × (smoothed size). So a long zero run drives p — and the forecast — toward zero, which is exactly the behaviour you want for items going obsolete. TSB is also theoretically unbiased at an arbitrary point in time, unlike Croston.

Design a demand forecasting system for 50,000 SKU×location series, refreshed weekly.
Expert▾

Layers: (1) ingestion — sales, on-hand, price, promo, master data, snapped to one grain with calendar alignment and structural-zero handling; (2) feature store — lags, rolling stats, future-known covariates, shared by train and serve; (3) segmentation/routing — ADI/CV² classify into smooth/intermittent/lumpy/new, route to GBM / Croston-SBA-TSB / analogue; (4) training & selection — rolling-origin CV picks a champion per segment, logged for reproducibility; (5) reconciliation — MinT/BU so SKU→channel→zone→national is coherent; (6) serving — batch write of forecast + intervals to the planning table, versioned; (7) monitoring — realised WMAPE/bias by segment, input drift, coverage, with retrain triggers. Name failure modes per stage: late/dirty data, training/serving skew, leakage, drift, incoherence.

How would you design promo-aware forecasting end to end?
Advanced▾

Get the promo calendar as a first-class, future-known input (mechanic, depth, display, price). Two viable designs: a single model with promo/price/display features (simpler, lets the model learn interactions), or a baseline+uplift split (forecast non-promo baseline, separately model uplift/elasticity, recombine — more interpretable for commercial teams). Either way, hold out whole promo events in backtesting to measure promo accuracy specifically, track post-promo dip, and expose a what-if endpoint so planners can test a promo plan before committing. Guard against the model attributing organic growth to promos (confounding).

Design a champion/challenger framework for forecasting models.
Advanced▾

The champion serves production; challengers run in shadow on the same data. Each cycle, score all models on rolling-origin CV by segment with WMAPE/bias/MASE, plus a stability check across folds. Promote a challenger only if it beats the champion by a meaningful, consistent margin (not one lucky fold), and add hysteresis so you don't flip back and forth on noise. Keep an immutable log of which model served which forecast. Optionally A/B at the segment level so a challenger can win on intermittent items while the champion holds the dense ones.

How would you design the backtesting harness itself?
Advanced▾

Make rolling-origin the only path to a score — no single-split shortcuts. Parameterise window type (expanding vs sliding), horizon, step size, and number of folds. Compute features inside each fold to prevent leakage. Emit per-fold, per-horizon, per-segment metrics (WMAPE/bias/MASE) plus interval coverage for probabilistic models, and always include seasonal-naive as a built-in baseline column. Persist results keyed by model+data version so comparisons are reproducible. The harness should make the right thing (proper CV against a baseline) the easy thing.

Design forecasting for a hierarchy where leaves are intermittent.
Expert▾

This is genuinely hard: intermittent leaves aggregate into smoother parents, and point reconciliation can distort the sparse bottom. A pragmatic design: forecast leaves with intermittent methods (TSB/SBA) and aggregate levels with smooth models, then reconcile — but prefer probabilistic reconciliation so the leaf distributions (not just point values) cohere, and validate that reconciled leaf forecasts still make sense for ordering. Often the decision at the leaf is driven by an inventory policy on the distribution rather than the point forecast, so optimise the end inventory metric, not just coherence. Flag this as an area where you'd benchmark carefully rather than assume MinT-on-points is correct.

How do you design the new-product cold-start to live-model transition?
Advanced▾

Define phases by data availability. Phase 0 (pre-launch): analogue or attribute-based forecast, or category top-down split. Phase 1 (weeks 1–N): blend analogue with an emerging fitted/foundation-model forecast using weights that shift from analogue→model as history grows, so there's no visible jump. Phase 2: graduate to the standard segment model once enough history exists to pass its own backtest. Make the phase and method visible to planners (so they trust it), and review analogue choices, since a bad analogue dominates the early forecast.

Design monitoring and alerting for a fleet of forecasting models.
Advanced▾

Track three signal families with segment granularity: accuracy (realised WMAPE/bias/MASE vs the seasonal-naive floor), data health (feed completeness, freshness, schema/distribution drift), and operations (job success, coverage, latency). Alert on actionable conditions — a segment dropping below the naive baseline, a sustained bias breach, a feed missing, coverage gaps — not on every wobble. Provide a dashboard sliced by channel/zone/velocity so a planner can see where it's failing, and tie an alert to a runbook (retrain, fix feed, investigate drift) rather than just a notification.

How would you combine multiple models into an ensemble in production?
Advanced▾

Start simple: a plain average of a few decent models is one of the most reliable accuracy gains in forecasting and is robust and easy to explain. If you want weights, learn them on validation folds (e.g. inverse-error or constrained regression), but regularise toward equal weights to avoid overfitting the combination. Combine at the level where it helps (often per segment), keep seasonal-naive in the mix as a stabiliser, and re-estimate weights on a schedule. Always check the ensemble beats its best member on rolling-origin CV — sometimes it doesn't.

Design a forecasting setup that feeds an inventory optimisation system.
Expert▾

The inventory system needs lead-time demand distributions, not point forecasts. Design the forecaster to output quantiles (or full predictive distributions) over the relevant lead time per SKU×location, calibrated (conformal) so coverage is honest. Pass the distribution downstream so safety stock and order-up-to levels derive from real uncertainty and the target service level. Close the loop: measure the forecaster by the resulting fill rate, stock-outs, and holding cost — not just WMAPE — because a point-accurate forecast with mis-stated uncertainty still produces bad inventory.

How do you design forecasting to support S&OP across channels and zones?
Advanced▾

S&OP demands one number that's coherent across the planning hierarchy and reconciled with commercial intent. Design a base statistical forecast at the operating grain, reconcile up the channel/zone/national tree (MinT/BU) so every level agrees, then provide a managed consensus layer where commercial overrides are captured with reason codes and measured for value-added. Surface accuracy and bias by the dimensions leadership cares about (channel, zone, brand), and keep the statistical baseline visible alongside the consensus so the meeting argues about the delta, not the raw numbers.

Design a build-vs-buy decision for forecasting capability.
Intermediate▾

Frame it on data, differentiation, and talent. Buy/adopt open libraries (Nixtla, Darts) for the modelling core — reinventing AutoARIMA or MinT adds no value. Build the parts specific to your business: data integration, segmentation/routing logic, promo handling, reconciliation rules, and the planner-facing consensus and monitoring. A packaged demand-planning suite buys speed and integration but constrains method flexibility and the foundation-model frontier; a built stack on open libraries gives control at the cost of engineering. Most mature teams land on "buy the platform plumbing, build the modelling and business logic on open components."

What is the Kalman filter actually doing inside ETS and ARIMA?
Advanced▾

Both can be written as linear Gaussian state-space models: a hidden state (level, trend, seasonal) evolves over time and you observe a noisy function of it. The Kalman filter recursively estimates that hidden state — it predicts the next state and its covariance, then updates when a new observation arrives, weighting prediction vs observation by their relative variances (the Kalman gain). Forecasts and their prediction intervals fall out of propagating the state and its covariance forward. This unifying view is why ETS, ARIMA, and structural/unobserved-components models all give native intervals and can be composed (level + trend + seasonal + regression) in one framework.

What is the Theta method and why should I keep it around?
Intermediate▾

Theta decomposes a series into "theta lines" (second differences scaled by a factor θ), forecasts each, and recombines; the standard variant is equivalent to simple exponential smoothing with drift. It's almost free to compute yet won the M3 competition and remains a top-tier benchmark. Keep it in your baseline set alongside seasonal-naive and ETS — if a complex model can't beat Theta on rolling-origin CV, the complexity isn't earning its keep.

My daily series has both weekly and yearly seasonality — what do I use?
Intermediate▾

Single-seasonal ETS/ARIMA can only represent one cycle, so they'll miss one. Use a multiple-seasonality method: MSTL (iterated STL across periods — the fast robust default), TBATS (trigonometric seasonality, handles long/non-integer periods), Prophet (additive trend + Fourier seasonality + holidays), or simply add Fourier (sin/cos) harmonics for each period as features in a GBM. For daily retail, MSTL with season lengths [7, 365] or Fourier terms in your LightGBM are the pragmatic choices.

What does DeepAR output, and why is a negative-binomial likelihood good for retail?
Advanced▾

DeepAR is a global autoregressive RNN that, at each step, outputs the parameters of a likelihood rather than a point. You forecast by sampling forward many times and reading quantiles off the trajectory distribution. The negative-binomial output models over-dispersed counts — where variance exceeds the mean, which is exactly how unit sales behave — far better than a Gaussian, which would assign probability to impossible negative demand and mis-state the spread. It's a strong choice when you want native probabilistic count forecasts across many related series.

N-BEATS vs N-HiTS — what's the difference?
Advanced▾

N-BEATS is a deep stack of fully-connected blocks with a doubly-residual design: each block emits a backcast (subtracted from the input) and a forecast (added to the output), with no recurrence or attention. N-HiTS adds multi-rate input sampling and hierarchical interpolation so different stacks specialise in different frequencies, making it more accurate and much cheaper on long horizons. Both are excellent univariate workhorses; reach for N-HiTS when horizons are long or compute matters.

When would you actually choose TFT over LightGBM?
Advanced▾

When you have many long series, a rich mix of static + time-varying-known + time-varying-unknown covariates, abundant data and compute, and you value the variable-selection and attention interpretability TFT provides (and native quantile outputs). On typical tabular FMCG panels, a tuned LightGBM frequently ties TFT at a fraction of the cost, so the honest answer is: benchmark both on rolling-origin CV and confirm any TFT edge is statistically significant (Diebold-Mariano) before taking on the operational burden. TFT earns its place on data-rich, covariate-heavy problems, not by default.

What is CRPS and how does it relate to pinball loss?
Expert▾

CRPS (Continuous Ranked Probability Score) is a proper scoring rule for a full predictive distribution: CRPS(F,y) = ∫ (F(z) − 𝟙{z≥y})² dz. It's "proper" because it's minimised by the true distribution, so it can't be gamed by over/under-dispersing, and it's in demand units (it generalises MAE — a point forecast's CRPS is just absolute error). Crucially, averaging the pinball (quantile) loss over a dense grid of quantile levels approximates the CRPS, so if you train a multi-quantile model and average its pinball losses, you're effectively estimating CRPS. For coherent multivariate/hierarchical forecasts, the energy score extends CRPS to vectors. The M5 Uncertainty track scored this family.

My model's 90% intervals only cover ~70% of actuals. How do I fix it?
Advanced▾

Model-native intervals are typically over-confident because they ignore parameter and structural uncertainty. Wrap the forecaster in conformal prediction, which calibrates intervals on held-out residuals to hit nominal coverage. For time series you need the adaptive variants (classical conformal assumes exchangeability, which time series break): conformalized quantile regression (CQR) if you already have quantile forecasts, or online methods like ACI/EnbPI that adapt to drift. Then always re-verify empirical coverage and report it — never trust nominal levels, and prefer the sharpest calibrated interval, not the narrowest.

Why does classical conformal prediction fail on time series?
Advanced▾

Classical (split) conformal's coverage guarantee rests on exchangeability — the calibration and test points being interchangeable in distribution. Time series violate this in two ways: temporal dependence (today correlates with yesterday) and distribution shift (the process drifts). So a naive conformal interval calibrated on the past can systematically miscover the future. The adaptive families restore validity by different mechanisms: weighting recent residuals (WCP), refreshing the residual pool (EnbPI), adapting the target coverage online via feedback (ACI), or blocking the data (Block-CP). They trade the exact finite-sample guarantee for long-run/asymptotic coverage under weak dependence.

CQR vs ACI vs EnbPI — which conformal method when?
Advanced▾

CQR (conformalized quantile regression) wraps an existing quantile model and adapts interval width to heteroscedasticity — use it when you already produce quantiles (TFT, LightGBM-quantile). EnbPI is bootstrap-ensemble based and slides its residual pool forward without data splitting — good for ensembles and streaming multi-step. ACI adapts the target miscoverage level online via feedback, so it self-corrects under abrupt distribution shift — use it when drift is real and you want long-run coverage. In practice many teams combine: CQR for adaptive width plus an online controller (ACI) for drift. Libraries like MAPIE and Nixtla implement these.

How does the Diebold-Mariano test work, and when do I need it?
Advanced▾

It tests whether two forecasts have equal expected accuracy. You form the loss differential dₜ = L(e₁ₜ) − L(e₂ₜ) for any loss (absolute, squared, pinball, CRPS) and test whether its mean is zero; the statistic d̄/√Var̂(d̄) is asymptotically N(0,1), with the variance computed by an autocorrelation-robust estimator and a Harvey-Leybourne-Newbold small-sample correction for multi-step. You need it whenever you're about to declare one model "better" — a 0.31 vs 0.33 WMAPE gap may be within noise, and DM tells you if the difference is real. For many models across many series, use rank-based MCB/Nemenyi tests instead.

How do I know whether a series is even forecastable?
Expert▾

Some series are essentially noise around a mean and no model will beat naive — measure forecastability before investing. Useful signals: the coefficient of variation and ADI (demand pattern), and spectral entropy — the entropy of the series' normalised power spectrum, near 1 for white noise (unpredictable) and near 0 when energy concentrates at a few frequencies (predictable/seasonal). Operationally, route low-forecastability SKUs to a cheap robust method (naive/Croston) and spend model budget on the forecastable ones; trying to "improve accuracy" on inherently random series is wasted effort and invites overfitting.

Why is the mean forecast usually the wrong number to order to?
Advanced▾

Because the cost-optimal order is the newsvendor critical-fractile quantile, not the mean. If under-stocking costs Cu and over-stocking costs Co per unit, the optimal order is the demand quantile at Cu/(Cu+Co). A high-margin item where stock-outs hurt (Cu ≫ Co) should be ordered to a high quantile (e.g. P95) — you deliberately over-order; a perishable with steep markdown (high Co) to a low quantile. Ordering the mean stocks out roughly half the time. This is why you should forecast the relevant quantile per SKU, and why pinball loss at that quantile equals the inventory cost — minimising the right quantile loss is minimising expected cost.

How do I forecast a brand-new product with the Bass model?
Advanced▾

The Bass diffusion model describes new-product adoption as an S-curve driven by innovators (coefficient p, external influence) and imitators (coefficient q, word-of-mouth), scaled to market potential m. With no history you set p, q, m from analogues (similar past launches) and update as real sales arrive. Pair it with analogue forecasting (clone and scale a similar SKU's launch curve), attribute-based regression (predict first-year demand from pack size, price tier, brand), and structured judgment. Design the system to blend analogue → fitted model over the first 8–12 weeks so there's no visible jump when enough history accrues.

My stock-out-prone items keep under-forecasting and under-stocking. Why?
Advanced▾

Censored demand. Your data records sales, not demand; when an item stocks out, sales are capped below true demand. Train on sales as if they were demand and the model learns availability, perpetually under-forecasting items that stock out — a self-reinforcing loop. Fix it by demand unconstraining: flag stock-out periods, treat their sales as a lower bound, and recover latent demand (classically via Expectation-Maximisation, or a censored/Tobit likelihood). Even simply dropping or imputing known stock-out weeks before fitting beats ignoring the censoring.

What actually changed with foundation models in 2025–2026?
Advanced▾

Three things. A shared benchmark (GIFT-Eval) made claims comparable. A second generation arrived — notably Chronos-2, which finally handles covariates and multivariate inputs via in-context group attention, plus Moirai-2.0/MoE, TimesFM-2.x, Toto, Sundial, and efficiency models like TinyTimeMixer. And the community got more honest about caveats: benchmark leakage inflates some reported gains (they shrink on genuinely unseen data), and zero-shot probabilistic outputs aren't always well-calibrated. Net: foundation models are an excellent instant baseline, cold-start tool, and ensemble member; for covariate-rich retail a tuned GBM — or a covariate-aware foundation model, fine-tuned — is still typically where you land.
