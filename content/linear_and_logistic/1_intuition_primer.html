Big idea 1

Two machines, one engine

🧒 Base camp→🎓 Trail→🔬 Summit
🧒 Explain like I'm 10

Picture a scoring machine. You feed it clues — how cheap something is, how big the discount, how hot the day — and it adds them all up into one score, giving each clue more or less importance.

Now there are two versions of this machine with different gadgets bolted on the end. The first one just prints the score as a number on a receipt: "this'll sell about 150 packets." The second one runs the score into a dimmer dial that shows a chance: fully off means "no," fully on means "yes," and the dial can land anywhere in between — "73% likely." Same adding-up guts inside. Different gadget on the end.

Recipe score: points for sweetness, crunch, price → one tastiness number.
Spam light: a pile of email clues squished into "probably spam."
Loan decision: same clue-adding, output is "chance you'll repay."
The fork — one score, two gadgets
The dots flowing along the wires = each clue carrying its value into the adder. The single score then either gets printed (linear) or turned into a chance (logistic).
🎓 One step up the trail

The two machines are linear regression (number out) and logistic regression (probability out). The shared engine — the weighted sum of the inputs — is called the linear score, written z = β₀ + β₁x₁ + β₂x₂ + … (the β's are the "importance" of each clue). Linear regression reports z directly; logistic regression bends z into a 0–1 chance. Learn the engine once and you've half-learned both models.

Big idea 2

Drawing the best line

🧒 Base camp→🎓 Trail→🔬 Summit
🧒 Explain like I'm 10

Sprinkle dots on a page: each dot is one week — how big the discount was (across) and how much you sold (up). They roughly trend upward but they're messy.

Now lay a ruler across them. You're not trying to hit any single dot perfectly — you're trying to position the ruler so the total leftover gap between the dots and the line is as small as possible. Some dots end up a little above the line, some a little below, but overall it's the "fairest" straight line through the cloud. That line is the model. Slide a new discount along the bottom, read the line's height, and that's your prediction.

Trend line: the straight line a spreadsheet draws through a scatter of points.
Fitness app: the smooth trend through your bouncy weekly weigh-ins.
Height guess: predicting an adult's height from a cloud of family data.
The fairest line through messy dots
Blue dots are real weeks; the teal line is the model. The red sticks are the misses — the line is placed to make their total (squared) length as small as possible.
🎓 One step up the trail

This is linear regression, fitted by Ordinary Least Squares (OLS). The red gaps are residuals; "smallest total gap" means minimising the sum of their squares (squaring makes big misses count extra and keeps the math smooth). The line's tilt for each input is that input's weight — and with one input there's a tidy formula that lands the line in one shot.

Big idea 3

Walking downhill in fog

🧒 Base camp→🎓 Trail→🔬 Summit
🧒 Explain like I'm 10

How does the machine find the best ruler position? Imagine standing on a foggy hillside, wanting to reach the lowest point in the valley. You can't see far — but you can feel which way the ground slopes down under your feet. So you take a step that way. Then feel again, step again. Keep going and you end up at the bottom.

For the machine, "height" is how wrong it is, and each step tweaks the knobs to be a little less wrong. Step too big and you bound across the valley and overshoot; step too small and it takes forever — but get it right and you settle at the bottom: the least-wrong setting.

Shower tap: too hot → nudge cold → overshoot → nudge back, until just right.
Tuning by ear: sharpen/flatten a string toward the right pitch, bit by bit.
A marble in a bowl: it always rolls to the bottom and rests there.
Rolling down to the least-wrong spot
The red ball = the model's current guess. Feeling the slope, it steps downhill until it rests at the bottom — the setting with the least error. (If the animation is off, the ball simply sits partway down the slope it's descending.)
🎓 One step up the trail

This is gradient descent. "Feeling the slope" is computing the gradient; the step size is the learning rate. Here's the lucky part: for both linear and logistic regression the hill is a single smooth bowl (mathematicians say convex) — no fake valleys to get trapped in — so you're guaranteed to reach the true bottom every time. That's why these models train fast and give the same answer on every run, unlike fussier methods such as neural networks.

Big idea 4

The squish into a yes/no chance

🧒 Base camp→🎓 Trail→🔬 Summit
🧒 Explain like I'm 10

Sometimes you don't want a number — you want "how likely is yes?" But the engine's raw score can be anything: minus a hundred, plus a thousand. A chance has to live between 0% and 100% and never escape.

So we slide the score through a squisher — a smooth S-shaped ramp. Very negative scores get flattened down to "almost certainly no" (near 0). Very positive scores flatten up to "almost certainly yes" (near 1). And there's a gentle middle where the chance swings fastest. It's like a dimmer switch that physically can't go past fully-on or fully-off, no matter how hard you push the dial.

Confidence meter: caps at 100% however sure you get.
Volume knob: can't go below mute or above max.
Thermostat: "basically off" → smooth middle → "basically on."
The S-shaped squisher
The dot riding the curve = a score being turned into a chance. Notice the flat ends: once the model is near-certain, an even bigger score barely nudges the chance.
🎓 One step up the trail

This is logistic regression, and the squisher is the sigmoid, σ(z) = 1/(1+e⁻ᶻ). Because of the flat ends, the model really speaks in odds rather than flat percentages — a coefficient changes the odds of "yes," which moves the probability a lot in the steep middle and barely at all out at the confident ends. Turn a chance into a hard yes/no only at the very end, with a cutoff you choose to fit the stakes.

Big idea 5

What the knobs are telling you

🧒 Base camp→🎓 Trail→🔬 Summit
🧒 Explain like I'm 10

Inside the machine is a row of knobs — one per clue. A big knob means "this clue matters a lot." A knob near zero means "basically ignore this." And a knob turned the other way (negative) means "this clue pushes the answer down."

Read the knobs and you get the whole story in plain words: "deep discount → way more sales; rainy day → a few less; day-of-week → barely matters." This is the machine's superpower: unlike a mysterious black box, it can tell you why it said what it said — which is exactly why banks, hospitals and planners trust it.

Judge's scorecard: each category weighted by how much it counts.
Nutrition math: calories = carbs×4 + protein×4 + fat×9 — fat's knob is biggest.
Loan checklist: points added or subtracted per factor.
Reading the importance knobs
Each bar is one knob (coefficient). Right = pushes the answer up, left = pushes it down, length = how much. The model hands you this story for free.
🎓 One step up the trail

The knobs are the coefficients (the weights β). In linear regression a weight reads as "this many more units of output per one unit of this input." In logistic regression it's about odds — you flip it into an odds ratio (e^weight), which reads as "each unit multiplies the odds of yes by this much." Either way, the model is interpretable: the knobs are the explanation.

Big idea 6 · the real world bites

Where it trips up in real life

🧒 Base camp→🎓 Trail→🔬 Summit
🧒 Explain like I'm 10

The machine is honest but a little naive. Four classic ways it gets fooled:

  • It only knows what it's seen. Show it discounts up to 30%, then ask about a wild 70%-off blowout, and it'll confidently invent an answer — sometimes even "negative sales." It can't say "I've never seen that."
  • It can memorise instead of learn. Give it too many knobs and too few examples and it'll ace the practice data and faceplant on anything new — like a student who memorised last year's exam.
  • It can cheat by accident. If one clue secretly contains the answer (a column only filled in after an order already shipped late), it looks like a genius in testing and is useless live.
  • It can brag about rare things. If only 2 in 100 orders go late, a lazy machine that always says "not late" is "98% correct" while catching zero real problems.
Crammer: memorises past papers, flunks the real test (overfitting).
Summer-only weather app: clueless about snow (extrapolation).
"98% right": ...because it never rains anyway (imbalance).
Four ways it gets fooled
None of these are exotic — they're the everyday traps that turn a good-looking model into a misleading one. Spotting them is most of the skill.
🎓 One step up the trail

Their real names are extrapolation, overfitting, data leakage, and class imbalance. The fixes are equally real: regularisation (stop the machine over-trusting any one knob), splitting your data before you prepare it (so it can't peek), time-based splits, and judging rare-event models with the right scorecard (PR-AUC, recall) and a cost-based cutoff instead of plain accuracy. The Reference's Pitfalls section is a field guide to all of them.
