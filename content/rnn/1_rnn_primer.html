⏱ ~8 min · 🧒 gentle · animated

🧠 What if a network could remember?

A normal network looks at one thing and forgets it instantly. But a sentence, a song, or a month of sales only makes sense in order. An RNN reads things one step at a time and keeps a little running memory — a "gist" — that it carries forward.

That carried-forward memory is the entire idea. Everything else in this topic is just making that memory better.

🔁 One cell, used again and again

There isn't a long chain of different boxes — there's one little cell, applied to each new word, passing its memory along. Drawing a copy per word ("unrolling") is just a way to see the loop.

Why this is cleverOne small cell handles a sentence of any length — 5 words or 500 — with the same handful of knobs. That's the magic of reusing the same weights at every step.

🌫️ The forgetting problem

Plain RNNs have a flaw: the further back something happened, the more its influence fades. "I grew up in France… I speak fluent ___" needs memory from long ago — and a simple RNN has usually forgotten by then.

This is the villainThis fading is the vanishing-gradient problem. Fixing it is what the next idea — gates — was invented for.

🚪 Gates: a memory that lasts

The fix (the LSTM) adds a protected "memory highway" plus three tiny gates that decide what to forget, what to add, and what to reveal. Because the highway runs almost untouched, important things survive across long gaps.

In plain wordsAn LSTM is a notebook with a smart eraser, a pen, and a highlighter. Forget the irrelevant, jot the important, show only what matters now.

🔦 Learning where to look

To translate or summarise, the model maps one sequence to another. Early versions squeezed the whole input into one vector — and choked on long inputs. The breakthrough, attention, lets the model look back at every input word and focus on the relevant ones, fresh for each output word.

Why it mattered so muchAttention worked so well that researchers dropped the loop entirely and kept only attention — and invented the Transformer, the engine behind today's AI like ChatGPT. You just met its grandparent.

♻️ The memory comeback (2026)

Transformers took over — but they're expensive on very long inputs. So in 2026 the wheel turned: new models like Mamba and RWKV bring back RNN-style memory (cheap, streaming, constant-cost) while fixing the old problems. Recurrence is cool again.

The takeawayUnderstanding RNNs isn't history — it's exactly what you need to read the newest architectures. Memory never went out of style.
