CNNs — The Visual Primer

How does a computer go from a grid of pixels to "that's a cat"? No equations here — just pictures and plain language for eight big ideas. When you're hungry for the math and code, every idea has a ⬆ level-up link into the full Reference.

⏱ ~15 min read · 8 ideas · zero math

⬆ Coming from the Foundations primer? If you already met a plain neural network — neurons, weights, "learn by adjusting" — you have everything you need. A CNN is the same idea with one twist: instead of wiring every pixel to every neuron, it uses a tiny sliding stamp that reuses the same weights all over the image. That's the whole secret.
Big idea 1

An image is just a grid of numbers

To you, a picture is a face, a dog, a sunset. To a computer it's a spreadsheet of brightness values — one number per pixel (three numbers for colour: how red, how green, how blue). That's it. Every clever thing a CNN does starts from this grid of numbers.

So the real question becomes: how do you teach a machine to look at a grid of numbers and notice "there's an edge here" or "this looks like an eye"? That's what the next idea answers.

⬆ Level upThe Reference treats this grid as a tensor of shape (channels, height, width) and builds everything on it — see Reference §01.
Big idea 2

A filter is a little pattern-stamp that slides

A filter (also called a kernel) is a tiny stamp — say 3×3 — that holds a pattern, like "two bright vertical bars." You slide it across the whole image, and at each spot you ask: how well does what's underneath match my pattern? Where it matches, you get a high score; where it doesn't, a low one.

The magic: the stamp's pattern isn't designed by a human — the network learns it from examples. And because the same stamp is used everywhere, a feature it learns (an edge, a curve) is recognised no matter where it appears. One stamp, slid everywhere, gives you a whole new "where did this pattern show up?" map.

Why this is cleverReusing one small stamp across the image means far fewer things to learn than wiring up every pixel separately — and it means a cat in the corner is found by the very same stamp as a cat in the middle.
⬆ Level upThat "match score" is a dot product; sliding it is convolution. The Reference derives it with numbers in §03 and a full worked example in §18.
Big idea 3

Stack the stamps: edges → shapes → things

One layer of stamps finds simple stuff: edges, blobs, colours. Now feed those results into another layer of stamps. The second layer combines edges into corners and curves. A third combines those into eyes, wheels, letters. Stack enough layers and the last one is combining parts into whole objects.

Nobody tells the network "an eye is two curves and a circle." It discovers that hierarchy on its own, simply because stacking pattern-finders on top of pattern-finders is the cheapest way to explain what's in the training images. This simple → complex ladder is the heart of why deep networks work so well on images.

⬆ Level upSee the real spatial-shrinks-channels-grow pipeline and named architectures (ResNet, ConvNeXt) in Reference §06 and §12.
Big idea 4

Pooling is just squinting

After finding patterns, CNNs deliberately shrink the picture. The simplest way — max-pooling — chops the map into little blocks and keeps only the strongest value from each. It's exactly like squinting at a photo: the fine detail blurs away, but the important stuff (a bright edge here, a strong feature there) survives.

Why throw away detail on purpose? Two reasons. It cuts the amount of computation (smaller grid = less work), and it makes the network not care about a pixel or two of wobble — a feature that shifts slightly still lands in the same block. The network keeps the "what" and stops fussing over the exact "where."

⬆ Level upModern nets often swap pooling for strided convolutions and finish with one Global Average Pool — compared head-to-head in Reference §05.
Big idea 5

Why deeper layers "see" more

A stamp in the first layer only looks at a tiny 3×3 patch. But a stamp in the second layer looks at the outputs of the first — so it indirectly covers a bigger chunk of the original image. Go deeper and each neuron's "window onto the original photo" keeps growing. By the deep layers, a single neuron is effectively looking at most of the picture at once.

This is why depth matters: shallow neurons can only spot tiny local things; deep neurons can integrate a whole face or a whole car because their window has grown wide enough to contain it. The fancy name for "how much of the image a neuron sees" is the receptive field.

⬆ Level upThere's a clean formula for exactly how fast that window grows — see Reference §09.
Big idea 6

Skip connections: the express lane

Very deep networks have a problem: by the time information (and the learning signal) has squeezed through fifty layers, it can get faint and garbled — like a message passed down a long line of people. The fix is delightfully simple: add an express lane that lets the input skip straight past a couple of layers and rejoin further down.

Each block now only has to learn a small tweak to what came in, instead of rebuilding everything from scratch — and the learning signal has a clean shortcut straight back through the network. This one trick (called a residual or skip connection) is what let networks go from ~20 layers to hundreds, and it's in almost every modern vision model.

⬆ Level upThe "+1 gradient highway" math and the residual block diagram are in Reference §12.
Big idea 7

Transfer learning: borrow trained eyes

Training a big CNN from zero needs millions of images and serious hardware. Almost nobody does that. Instead you grab a model someone already trained on millions of photos — it has learned brilliant general-purpose "eyes" (edge, texture, and shape detectors) — freeze those, and only retrain the small final part to recognise your specific categories.

It's like hiring someone who already knows how to see and just teaching them your particular job. You can build a strong classifier for your problem — defective bottle caps, plant diseases, your cat vs the neighbour's — from a few hundred images in minutes, because the hard part was already done.

⬆ Level upFreeze-vs-fine-tune recipes and the modern weights API are in Reference §13; you'll do it hands-on in Project 4.
Big idea 8

Where CNNs trip up in the real world

CNNs are powerful but not wise. A classic failure: a "cow detector" that secretly learned to spot green grass, because every cow in its training photos stood on a field. It looks brilliant in testing — then meets a cow on a beach and falls apart. The model found a shortcut that correlated with cows instead of learning what a cow looks like.

Other trip-ups: a few oddly-placed pixels can fool them; they can quietly inherit biases from skewed training data; and they only know what they were shown — a brand-new angle or lighting can throw them. The lesson: always check what your model is actually keying on, and feed it varied, representative examples.

The honest takeawayA CNN doesn't "understand" images the way you do — it finds whatever patterns reliably predict the label in its training set, shortcuts included. Good data and a sanity-check of what it's looking at matter as much as the model.
⬆ Level upThe "look where the model looked" tool is Grad-CAM — explained in Reference §17 and built in Project 5.
